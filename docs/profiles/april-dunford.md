# April Dunford

> Design reference profile for Etak product discovery adaptation.

---

## Who They Are

April Dunford is a positioning consultant and author of *Obviously Awesome:
How to Nail Product Positioning so Customers Get It, Buy It, Love It* (2019)
and *Sales Pitch: How to Craft a Story to Stand Out and Win* (2023). She spent
25 years as a VP of Marketing at a series of tech startups before becoming an
independent consultant and one of the most sought-after positioning experts in
B2B technology.

Dunford is not a traditional PM thinker — she comes from the marketing and
go-to-market side. But her work is deeply relevant to product discovery because
positioning shapes which opportunities a team pursues, how they frame the
value they deliver, and which customers they target. Her core insight is that
most products don't fail because they are bad — they fail because customers
don't understand what they are or why they should care.

Key contributions:
- A rigorous, repeatable positioning framework
- The concept of "competitive alternatives" as the starting point for positioning
- The insight that positioning is context-setting, not messaging
- Practical methods for identifying your real market category
- *Sales Pitch* framework for translating positioning into a narrative

## Core Philosophy

Positioning is the most underrated activity in product. It is not messaging,
taglines, or branding — it is the fundamental act of defining the context in
which customers evaluate your product. Get the context wrong and the best
product in the world will fail. Get it right and a decent product can win.

The foundational question is: "What would customers do if your product didn't
exist?" The answer reveals your true competitive alternatives — not the
companies you think you compete with, but the actual alternatives customers
would use. This is often "do nothing," a spreadsheet, or a manual process.
Starting from competitive alternatives grounds your positioning in reality,
not aspiration.

Positioning follows a specific sequence:
1. **Competitive alternatives** — what would customers use if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — what do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value** — what do those attributes enable for the customer?
4. **Target customer** — who cares most about that value?
5. **Market category** — what context makes your value obvious?

This sequence is not negotiable. Each step depends on the one before it. You
cannot pick a market category first and then figure out your value — the
category is the *output* of the positioning process, not an input.

Market categories are cognitive shortcuts. When you say "CRM" or "project
management tool," customers instantly load a set of expectations about what
the product does, who it's for, and what it costs. Choosing the right
category is choosing the right set of expectations — the ones that make your
unique value obvious.

Positioning is not a one-time exercise. It must be revisited when the
competitive landscape changes, when you add significant new capabilities, or
when you expand to new customer segments. A product that was well-positioned
two years ago may be poorly positioned today.

## Discovery Vocabulary

**Signature terms and phrases:**
- "Competitive alternatives" — what customers would do if you didn't exist
- "Unique attributes" — capabilities or features that alternatives lack
- "Value" — what the unique attributes enable for the customer (not features)
- "Target customer" — the segment that cares most about your differentiated value
- "Market category" — the context that makes your value obvious
- "Positioning" — not messaging, but context-setting
- "Cognitive shortcut" — market categories as mental shortcuts for buyers
- "Differentiated value" — value that only you can deliver
- "Best-fit customer" — the customer who gets the most value from your
  differentiated capabilities
- "Trap" — being stuck in a market category that obscures your value

**Characteristic phrasing:**
- "What would your customers do if you didn't exist?"
- "You're trying to pick a market category before you understand your value.
  That's backwards."
- "Features are not value. Value is what features *enable*."
- "Who is your best-fit customer — not your total addressable market, but the
  people who love you and would be devastated if you disappeared?"
- "If you can't tell me why you're different from the alternative, neither
  can the customer."
- "You're confusing your vision with your positioning. Vision is where you're
  going. Positioning is where you are *right now*."
- "Stop positioning to the CEO's ambition. Position to the customer's reality."

**Metaphors:**
- Context as a frame around a painting — the frame changes what you see
- Market category as a shelf in a store — which aisle do you belong on?
- Competitive alternatives as the "real competition" (often surprisingly mundane)
- Positioning as setting the stage before the performance

## Questions They Ask

1. If your product disappeared tomorrow, what would your customers do instead? Don't say "a competitor" — be specific about the actual alternative.
2. What can you do that those alternatives genuinely cannot? Not "do better" — what can you do that they *can't* do?
3. So what? What does that unique capability actually enable for the customer? What value does it create in their life or work?
4. Who cares the most about that value? Not "everyone" — which specific segment gets the most outsized benefit?
5. What market category makes your differentiated value obvious? When you say "we're a ___," does the customer instantly understand why you matter?
6. Are you positioning based on what you are today, or based on where you want to be in two years? Those are different exercises.
7. Can your sales team articulate your positioning in a way that's consistent? If I asked five reps, would I get five different answers?
8. What assumptions about the market are baked into your current positioning? Which of those have changed since you last set it?
9. When prospects say "I don't get it," where exactly do they get confused? That's a positioning problem, not a product problem.
10. Are you competing in a market category where you can actually win, or are you in a category dominated by someone with more resources?
11. What is the switching cost for your best-fit customer? What are they giving up to adopt you?
12. Is your positioning driving your roadmap, or is your roadmap driving your positioning? (It should be the former.)

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Positioning by aspiration.** Choosing a market category based on where you
want to be, not where you are. "We're an AI platform" when you're really a
specialized tool for one use case. Aspirational positioning confuses
customers who need to understand what you do *now*.

**Feature-value confusion.** Listing features as if they are value. "We have
real-time sync" is a feature. "Your whole team is always working from the
same data" is value. Customers buy value, not features.

**Total addressable market thinking.** "Our target customer is everyone."
This is not a target — it is a refusal to choose. The best-fit customer
is specific, identifiable, and reachable.

**Category creation without leverage.** Trying to create a new market
category without the resources or credibility to educate the market.
Category creation is expensive. Most companies should position within
an existing category or a sub-category they can own.

**Competitive comparison with the wrong competitors.** Comparing yourself to
the venture-funded market leader when your real competition is
spreadsheets and email. Positioning against the wrong alternative makes
your value invisible.

**One-and-done positioning.** Setting positioning once and never revisiting
it. The market moves, your product evolves, customer needs shift.
Positioning is a living decision.

**Conflating positioning and messaging.** Positioning is the strategic
decision about context. Messaging is how you communicate that context.
You can't write good messaging without clear positioning, but many teams
skip straight to taglines.

**Inside-out thinking.** Defining your product based on what you built
instead of the problem you solve. "We're a platform that uses machine
learning to..." — the customer doesn't care about your technology stack.
They care about their problem.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team can state their competitive alternatives clearly, and the list
  includes the mundane alternatives (spreadsheets, manual processes, "do
  nothing") alongside actual product competitors.
- Unique attributes are genuinely unique — not "we're faster" or "we're
  easier to use," but specific capabilities that alternatives truly lack.
- Value is stated from the customer's perspective, not the company's. "You
  can..." not "We provide..."
- The target customer is specific enough that you could identify them in a
  room. Job title, company type, situation, trigger moment.
- The market category makes the value instantly legible. When someone says
  "we're a ___," the natural follow-up is "oh, interesting" not "what does
  that mean?"
- Positioning informs roadmap priorities. The team pursues opportunities
  that reinforce their differentiated value and deprioritizes things that
  dilute it.
- Sales and marketing can articulate the positioning consistently without
  reading from a script.
- Positioning is revisited regularly — at least when entering a new segment,
  launching a major capability, or when win/loss data shifts.

## Implications for Etak

**Positioning as discovery lens.** For a Dunford-aligned user, positioning is
not a downstream activity — it is a critical input to discovery. Etak should
ask positioning questions early: "Who is this for? What alternatives exist?
What's your differentiated value?" These questions filter which opportunities
are worth pursuing.

**Competitive alternatives as starting point.** When exploring opportunities,
Etak should push the user to name the actual alternatives customers use today.
This grounds the conversation in reality and prevents aspiration-driven
discovery.

**Value, not features.** When the user describes a solution, Etak should
consistently translate features into value. "You said 'real-time sync' —
what does that enable for the customer?" This is a core reflex for
Dunford-aligned thinking.

**Target customer specificity.** Etak should resist vague customer
definitions. When the user says "product teams," push for more: "Which
product teams? In what situation? At what company stage? What's the
trigger moment?"

**Positioning-roadmap alignment.** The opportunity solution tree should be
evaluated through a positioning lens. Does this opportunity reinforce
differentiated value? Or does it dilute positioning by chasing a feature
that doesn't serve the best-fit customer?

**Incremental formalism fit.** Moderate alignment. Dunford's framework is
already quite structured — the five-step sequence is specific and ordered.
But Etak can support the messy work of *figuring out* each step. The
dialogue might start loose ("tell me about your customers") and gradually
crystallize into the positioning components. Etak should know the framework
and help the user work through it without forcing premature structure.

**Market category as a late decision.** Etak should resist the urge to
categorize early. For a Dunford-aligned user, the market category is the
*last* positioning decision, not the first. Help them work through
alternatives, attributes, and value before asking about category.
