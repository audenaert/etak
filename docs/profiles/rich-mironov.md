# Rich Mironov

> Author of *The Art of Product Management*. "The PM Whisperer."
> Veteran product leader who focuses on the organizational and political
> realities of product work.

---

## Who They Are

Rich Mironov is a product management veteran with over 30 years of experience
as a PM, VP of Product, and interim CPO at dozens of companies. He wrote *The
Art of Product Management* (2008) and blogs prolifically at mironov.com. He is
often called "the PM whisperer" because companies bring him in as an interim
product leader to diagnose and fix product organizations.

His contribution is perspective — the battle-scarred, deeply pragmatic
perspective of someone who has seen every organizational failure mode and
understands why product management is hard in ways that books about frameworks
don't capture. He writes about the tension between what product management
*should* be and what it *actually is* inside real companies.

Where Torres teaches you how to do discovery well, and Cagan describes the
ideal product organization, Mironov tells you why your actual organization
won't let you do those things — and how to make progress anyway. He's the
person who validates your frustration and then helps you find the pragmatic
path forward.

## Core Philosophy

**Product management is organizational design.** The quality of product work
is determined more by organizational structure, incentives, and politics than
by the individual PM's skill with frameworks. A great PM inside a broken
organization will be ground down. The most important product decisions are
often made in conference rooms where no PM is present.

Mironov is a **realist without being a cynic**. He sees clearly that most
product organizations are dysfunctional — driven by sales commitments, exec
pet projects, and engineering-led roadmaps — and he doesn't pretend otherwise.
But he doesn't throw up his hands. He finds the pragmatic path: what can you
actually change, given the constraints you actually have?

He believes that **technical debt is product debt**. When the engineering team
is drowning in legacy code, that's not an engineering problem — it's a product
problem. The product can't evolve if the foundation is crumbling. PMs who
ignore technical health are borrowing from their own future.

The roadmap is a **political document**, not just a planning document. Every
roadmap makes implicit promises to stakeholders. Those promises create
expectations. Unmet expectations destroy trust. Mironov teaches PMs to
understand the political implications of what they put on the roadmap — and
what they leave off.

He has deep empathy for the individual PM. He understands that PMs are often
caught between what they know is right (invest in discovery, focus on outcomes)
and what the organization demands (ship this feature by Q3 because sales
already promised it). He doesn't judge. He helps.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Product management is organizational design** — the defining insight. How
  product decisions get made, who has authority, how priorities are set — these
  are organizational design problems, not methodology problems.
- **Revenue-driven roadmaps** — roadmaps dictated by sales commitments. "We
  promised Client X this feature by March." Mironov considers this the single
  most common dysfunction in B2B product organizations.
- **Technical debt as product debt** — when the codebase is brittle, slow, or
  unmaintainable, the product suffers. PMs must advocate for technical health
  as a product concern, not delegate it to engineering.
- **The roadmap is a political document** — every item on the roadmap is a
  promise. Every item left off is a disappointment. Understanding the politics
  of the roadmap is as important as understanding the strategy behind it.
- **Saying no** — the most important and hardest skill in product management.
  Saying yes is easy and popular. Saying no requires conviction, evidence,
  and political capital. Most PMs say yes too much.
- **Interim CPO** — Mironov's model of parachuting into organizations as a
  temporary product leader. Gives him a unique cross-company perspective on
  organizational patterns.
- **Sound product economics** — building products that can actually sustain
  a business. Not just user value, but unit economics, pricing, cost structure.
  Discovery must account for business viability, not just customer desirability.
- **The product org maturity spectrum** — organizations range from "sales-driven
  feature factories" to "outcome-oriented empowered teams." Most are somewhere
  in between, and progress is incremental.
- **Stakeholder translation** — the PM's job of translating between engineering
  ("we need to refactor the auth system"), sales ("the customer needs SSO"),
  and executives ("how does this affect ARR?"). Same reality, different
  languages.

## Questions They Ask

1. "Who actually decides what gets built here? Is it the PM, or is it
   sales/execs/engineering?"
2. "How much of the roadmap is committed to sales promises vs. discovery-
   driven opportunities?"
3. "What happens when you say no to a stakeholder? How does the
   organization react?"
4. "How much of engineering's capacity goes to technical debt vs. new
   features?"
5. "Does the executive team understand what product management is, or
   do they think it's project management?"
6. "What's the real constraint here — is it technical, organizational,
   or political?"
7. "Who are the stakeholders we need to bring along, and what do they
   care about?"
8. "Can this product actually make money at scale, or are we subsidizing
   growth with venture capital?"
9. "What did we promise customers that we can't deliver?"
10. "If I looked at where your PMs spend their time, how much is
    discovery vs. project coordination vs. stakeholder management?"
11. "What's the organizational incentive structure here? What gets
    rewarded?"
12. "Are we building features because customers need them, or because
    we committed to them in a contract?"
13. "What would happen if we didn't build this? Who would be upset,
    and does that matter?"
14. "How much of this 'product strategy' is actually a negotiated
    settlement between competing internal factions?"

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**The PM as project manager.** PMs who spend 80% of their time coordinating
delivery and 20% (or 0%) on discovery. This isn't a skill gap — it's an
organizational design failure. The org hasn't decided that discovery matters,
so PMs fill the coordination vacuum instead.

**Sales-driven roadmaps.** The most pernicious pattern in B2B. Sales closes
a deal with feature commitments. Those commitments become the roadmap. The
PM becomes an order-taker. Discovery dies because the backlog is already
full of promises. Mironov has seen this destroy dozens of product
organizations.

**Ignoring technical debt.** PMs who treat engineering's pleas for refactoring
as a distraction from "real" product work. Mironov insists that technical
health is product health. A team that can't ship quickly because of brittle
code is a product problem.

**Roadmap theater.** Beautiful roadmap presentations that everyone knows are
fiction. The dates will slip, the priorities will change, and the stakeholders
will be angry because they took the roadmap literally. Mironov pushes for
honest roadmaps that communicate uncertainty.

**Confusing motion with progress.** Shipping features is not the same as
making progress. If the features don't move metrics or serve validated needs,
you're just accumulating code. Mironov is allergic to feature velocity as a
success metric.

**Ignoring organizational constraints.** Idealistic approaches that work in
theory but fail in practice because they don't account for the politics,
incentives, and power dynamics of the actual organization. Mironov respects
the theory but insists on dealing with reality.

**The lone PM hero.** The idea that a single talented PM can fix a broken
product organization through sheer skill and determination. They can't. The
organization will win. Mironov pushes for systemic change, not heroics.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- PMs spend meaningful time on discovery, not just delivery coordination.
- The roadmap reflects strategic intent, not just sales commitments and
  executive pet projects.
- Technical debt gets regular, planned investment — not zero and not "we'll
  do it someday."
- PMs can say no to requests, with evidence and a clear alternative, and
  the organization accepts it.
- Stakeholders understand that the roadmap is directional, not a promise.
- PMs and engineering leads have a partnership based on mutual respect.
  Neither dominates.
- The organization measures product success by outcomes (retention, revenue,
  customer satisfaction), not output (features shipped).
- There's organizational learning — when a product bet fails, the
  organization adjusts, rather than pretending it didn't happen.

## Implications for Etak

**When a user thinks like Mironov, Etak should:**

- Acknowledge organizational reality. Mironov-style users know what
  "good" looks like but operate within constraints. Etak should help
  them navigate those constraints, not pretend the constraints don't
  exist. When a user says "we have to build this because sales promised
  it," Etak should help them make the best of that situation.
- Support the political dimension of discovery. Help users think about
  stakeholders, organizational dynamics, and how to build internal
  support for discovery-driven work. "Who needs to be on board for
  this opportunity to get investment?"
- Track the sources of roadmap items. Is this opportunity discovery-
  driven, sales-committed, exec-requested, or tech-debt-related?
  Making this visible helps the user understand their actual portfolio
  balance.
- Help frame discovery findings for different audiences. The same
  insight needs different framing for engineering, sales, and the
  executive team. Etak could help translate: "For the exec team, this
  is about retention risk. For engineering, it's about the auth system
  refactor."
- Support honest capacity planning. When the user adds opportunities
  to the tree, Etak might ask: "Given the commitments you've already
  described, is there actually capacity for this?"
- Integrate business viability into discovery. Mironov-style users
  won't be satisfied with "customers want this." They also need "and
  here's why it makes business sense." Etak should prompt for the
  economic dimension.

**Risks to manage:**

- Mironov's pragmatism, taken too far, becomes defeatism. "The org
  won't let us do real discovery, so why bother." Etak should honor
  the constraints while still pushing toward better practice. The
  question is always "what's the next step we *can* take?"
- His organizational focus can distract from the actual discovery
  work. Understanding the politics is necessary but not sufficient.
  You still have to talk to customers and test solutions.
- His perspective is heavily B2B-flavored. The sales-driven roadmap
  problem, the stakeholder translation challenge — these are more
  acute in enterprise contexts. Etak should calibrate for the user's
  actual environment.
