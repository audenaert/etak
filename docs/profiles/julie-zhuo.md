# Julie Zhuo

> Design reference profile for Etak product discovery adaptation.

---

## Who They Are

Julie Zhuo is a product and design leader, author of *The Making of a Manager*
(2019), and co-founder of Sundial. She spent over 14 years at Facebook/Meta,
rising from intern to VP of Design, where she led design for News Feed,
Groups, Events, and other core surfaces used by billions. She writes a popular
newsletter ("The Looking Glass") and is widely followed for her thinking on
design craft, decision-making under uncertainty, and leadership development.

Zhuo sits at the intersection of design and product. She is not a PM
methodology author — she is a design leader who thinks deeply about the
*quality of thinking* that precedes the quality of output. Her contributions
are less about frameworks and more about sensibility: how to develop taste,
how to run a design review that actually sharpens thinking, how to close the
gap between what you intended and what you actually shipped.

Key contributions:
- *The Making of a Manager* — a practical guide to management as craft
- "The Looking Glass" newsletter — essays on design, product, and leadership
- Thinking on design reviews as vehicles for collective reasoning
- Writing on taste, self-awareness, and the craft of making decisions

## Core Philosophy

Good product outcomes start with good thinking, and good thinking is a skill
you can develop. Most teams ship mediocre products not because they lack talent
but because they lack rigor in how they frame problems, evaluate options, and
make decisions.

The fundamental question is always: "What problem are we solving?" This sounds
simple, but most teams cannot answer it clearly. They jump to features,
timelines, and metrics without establishing a shared, precise understanding of
the problem. The problem statement is the foundation — if it's wrong, nothing
built on top of it will be right.

Design reviews are not presentations — they are thinking tools. A good design
review surfaces the decision being made, the options considered, the tradeoffs
involved, and the reasoning behind the recommendation. It should make everyone
in the room smarter about the problem, not just inform them of the solution.

Taste matters. Taste is not an innate gift — it is pattern recognition
developed through exposure, reflection, and practice. A person with taste can
look at a product and tell you what's wrong before they can explain why. This
intuition is valuable, but it must be combined with rigor — you need both the
instinct and the argument.

There is always a gap between intention and impact. What you meant to build
and what users experience are never the same thing. Closing that gap requires
humility, measurement, and a willingness to iterate even when you thought you
were done.

Self-awareness is the meta-skill. Knowing what you know, what you don't know,
and what your biases are is the foundation of good decision-making. The best
PMs and designers are the ones who can honestly assess the quality of their
own thinking.

## Discovery Vocabulary

**Signature terms and phrases:**
- "What problem are we solving?" — the foundational question, asked relentlessly
- "Intention vs. impact" — the gap between what you designed and what users
  experience
- "Taste" — developed intuition for what's good, built through practice
- "Design review" — not a presentation, but a structured thinking session
- "The Looking Glass" — self-reflection as a professional practice
- "Decision quality" — judging the quality of the process, not just the outcome
- "Confidence level" — how sure are we? What would change our mind?
- "Craft" — the discipline of doing work well, with attention to detail
- "Tradeoffs" — every decision has costs; the question is which costs you accept

**Characteristic phrasing:**
- "Wait — what problem are we solving here?"
- "What does success look like for the person using this?"
- "What are the top three options we considered, and why did we land here?"
- "How confident are we in this? What would need to be true for this to work?"
- "What's the simplest version of this that would tell us if we're right?"
- "I'm not sure we're being honest with ourselves about this tradeoff."
- "Can you walk me through the thinking, not just the conclusion?"

**Metaphors:**
- The looking glass — self-reflection as a practice
- The gap — the space between intention and impact
- Craft as a muscle — something that strengthens with deliberate practice
- Design reviews as sharpening stones — they refine thinking, not just present it

## Questions They Ask

1. What problem are we solving? Can you state it in one sentence without referencing the solution?
2. Who has this problem, and how do we know? What did we hear from them directly?
3. What does success look like from the user's perspective — not our metrics, but their experience?
4. What are the top three approaches we considered? Why did we choose this one over the others?
5. What tradeoffs are we making with this approach? What are we giving up?
6. How confident are we in this direction? What's our confidence level — high, medium, low? What would change it?
7. What's the simplest version of this that tests our core assumption?
8. If this ships and doesn't work, what's the most likely reason it failed?
9. What is the gap between our intention and what users will actually experience? Where will the gap be widest?
10. Are we designing for the first-time experience or the hundredth-time experience? Which matters more here?
11. If I showed this to the user without any explanation, would they understand what to do? Would they want to?
12. What does our taste tell us about this? Does it feel right, even if we can't fully articulate why?
13. Are we solving this problem because it's important, or because it's the one we know how to solve?
14. What would a great version of this look like? Not "what can we ship" — what would be genuinely great?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Solution-first thinking.** Jumping to "what should we build?" before
establishing a clear, shared understanding of the problem. The feature is
discussed before the problem is articulated. Roadmaps full of solutions
without problem statements.

**Design review as performance.** Presenting polished mockups for approval
rather than using the review to sharpen thinking. The review becomes a
check-the-box ceremony rather than a collaborative reasoning session.

**Metrics without meaning.** Optimizing numbers without asking whether the
numbers represent genuine user value. Engagement metrics that mask problems.
Vanity metrics that feel good but don't reflect the user's experience.

**False confidence.** Proceeding with high conviction on thin evidence. Not
calibrating confidence levels honestly. "We know what users want" based on
one interview or one executive's intuition.

**Lack of self-awareness.** Not recognizing your own biases, blind spots, or
the ways your preferences differ from users' needs. Designing for yourself
instead of for the person with the problem.

**Skipping the "why."** Moving straight from data to action without the
interpretive step. The data says X — but *why* does it say X? What does
it mean? Teams that collect data but don't build understanding.

**Perfectionism that prevents learning.** Polish and craft applied at the
wrong moment. Spending weeks on high-fidelity designs before validating
the direction. Taste is important, but timing matters — rough is fine
when you're still learning.

**Consensus-seeking.** Avoiding hard tradeoffs by trying to make everyone
happy. Good design requires making clear choices, and clear choices mean
some people won't get what they want.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- Every project can be traced back to a clearly stated problem. The problem
  statement is written down, shared, and referenced throughout the work.
- Design reviews are substantive. People come prepared to discuss tradeoffs
  and alternatives, not just react to mockups. The question "what else did
  you consider?" has a real answer.
- The team has calibrated confidence. They know what they're sure about and
  what they're guessing. They adjust their process accordingly — high
  confidence means move fast, low confidence means test first.
- Intention-impact gaps are tracked. After shipping, the team looks at
  whether the user's actual experience matches what they designed for.
  When gaps exist, they dig into why.
- Taste is cultivated. Team members study great products, discuss what makes
  them great, and develop shared language for quality. This is not
  aesthetic preference — it is informed judgment about what's good.
- Self-awareness is practiced. Retrospectives are honest. People can
  describe their own biases and blind spots. The team has mechanisms for
  challenging its own assumptions.
- Simplicity is valued. The simplest version that solves the problem is
  preferred. Complexity is added deliberately, with clear justification.
- The team can articulate tradeoffs. Not "we chose X" but "we chose X over
  Y because Z, and the cost is W."

## Implications for Etak

**Problem-first dialogue.** When a Zhuo-aligned user starts talking about
solutions, Etak should gently redirect to the problem. "Before we explore
solutions — can you state the problem in one sentence?" This is the single
most important intervention for this profile.

**Thinking quality over artifact quality.** This user cares about the rigor
of the reasoning, not the polish of the artifact. Etak should support
structured reasoning: "What options did you consider? What are the
tradeoffs? How confident are you?" The opportunity tree is a thinking
tool, not a deliverable.

**Confidence calibration.** Etak should regularly prompt for confidence
levels. "How confident are you in this opportunity? What evidence would
change your mind?" This helps the user practice the calibration Zhuo
values.

**Design review mode.** Etak could offer a "review" mode where it plays the
role of a thoughtful design reviewer: asking about alternatives
considered, tradeoffs made, and the reasoning behind choices. This
mirrors Zhuo's belief that reviews sharpen thinking.

**Intention-impact tracking.** When users define solutions, Etak should help
them articulate the intended experience — and later prompt them to
compare intention with reality. This closes the gap Zhuo writes about.

**Incremental formalism fit.** Strong alignment with Etak's core philosophy.
Zhuo-aligned users will want to think before they formalize. Etak should
support loose exploration ("tell me about the problem space") before
pushing toward structured artifacts. The crystallization should feel like
the natural result of clear thinking, not a bureaucratic requirement.

**Taste as a valid input.** When this user says "this doesn't feel right,"
Etak should treat that as meaningful signal and help them articulate
what's driving the instinct. Taste is not hand-waving — it's compressed
experience.
