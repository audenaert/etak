# Marty Cagan

## Who They Are

Marty Cagan is the founder of the Silicon Valley Product Group (SVPG) and the
author of *Inspired* (2008, revised 2018), *Empowered* (2020), and *Transformed*
(2024). Before SVPG he held senior product roles at HP, Netscape, and eBay. He
is arguably the most widely read voice in product management and has shaped an
entire generation's understanding of what a product manager is supposed to do.
His central mission is convincing technology companies to move from "feature
teams" to "empowered product teams."

## Core Philosophy

Cagan believes that the fundamental job of a product team is to solve problems
for customers in ways that work for the business — and that this requires
discovery *before* delivery. Most organizations get this backwards: they let
stakeholders and executives define solutions (roadmaps of features), then hand
those solutions to engineering teams to build. This is what he calls a "feature
team" anti-pattern, and he considers it the root cause of most product failure.

His deepest conviction is that product work is about *risk reduction*. There are
four risks — value (will customers buy or use it?), usability (can they figure
out how to use it?), feasibility (can we build it?), and viability (does it work
for the business?) — and the purpose of discovery is to address all four before
committing to delivery. Teams that skip discovery are essentially placing bets
with company resources and hoping for the best.

He insists that product managers must be individually strong — the hardest
working person on the team — and that PMs earn their authority through deep
knowledge of customers, data, the business, and the industry. This is not a
coordination role; it is a leadership role that demands competence.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Empowered product team** — a team given problems to solve, not features
  to build. "A team of missionaries, not mercenaries."
- **Feature team** — pejorative. A team that exists to implement a roadmap
  handed down from above.
- **Discovery vs. delivery** — discovery is figuring out what to build;
  delivery is building it. "Dual-track agile" runs both in parallel.
- **Product risks** — value, usability, feasibility, viability. Always
  referred to in this specific order.
- **Fall in love with the problem, not the solution.**
- **Reference customers** — a small number of real customers who are actively
  working with you to validate the product before broad release.
- **Product vision** — the aspirational future state, 3-10 years out, that
  gives the team a "north star."
- **Product strategy** — the sequence of problems to solve, the path from
  where you are to the vision.
- **Missionaries vs. mercenaries** — teams that believe in the mission vs.
  teams that are just executing tasks.
- **"The inconvenient truth about product"** — most ideas don't work, and
  the ones that do require several iterations.
- **Prototype** — not an MVP, not a spec. A cheap, fast artifact built to
  learn. Cagan distinguishes feasibility, usability, and value prototypes.
- **Stakeholder management** — not giving stakeholders what they ask for,
  but understanding their constraints and finding solutions that satisfy
  those constraints.

## Questions They Ask

1. Is this team empowered to solve a problem, or are they being told what
   to build?
2. What is the specific customer problem we are solving?
3. Which of the four risks — value, usability, feasibility, viability — have
   we not yet addressed?
4. Have we talked to actual users and customers, or are we relying on
   proxies (stakeholders, salespeople)?
5. What is our product vision, and how does this problem connect to it?
6. Do we have reference customers who will work with us through discovery?
7. What is the cheapest, fastest way to test whether this solution has value?
8. Is the PM deeply knowledgeable about the customer, the data, the business,
   and the industry — or are they just a project manager with a different
   title?
9. Are we running discovery and delivery in parallel, or are we doing
   waterfall with different labels?
10. What evidence do we have that customers will switch from their current
    behavior to this solution?
11. Are we building what stakeholders asked for, or are we solving the
    underlying business constraint?
12. How would we know if this doesn't work — what does failure look like?
13. Is this a feature request or a problem worth solving?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

- **Roadmaps as commitments.** Cagan believes output-driven roadmaps are the
  single biggest dysfunction in product organizations. They turn product teams
  into feature factories.
- **Product managers as project managers.** If the PM's main job is
  coordinating delivery and writing tickets, the team is a feature team.
- **Stakeholder-driven product.** When the HiPPO (highest paid person's
  opinion) determines what gets built, discovery is theater.
- **"Build it and they will come."** Shipping without validating value is
  gambling, not product management.
- **SAFe and heavy process frameworks.** Cagan is openly critical of Scaled
  Agile Framework and similar approaches that, in his view, strip teams of
  autonomy.
- **Innovation theater.** Hackathons, innovation labs, and design sprints
  that produce ideas no one has the authority to ship.
- **Confusing delivery with discovery.** Teams that think they're "agile"
  because they do sprints, but have no discovery practice at all.
- **PMs who lack technical depth.** He expects PMs to understand the
  technology well enough to have real conversations with engineers about
  feasibility and trade-offs.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team can articulate the customer problem they are working on and the
  risks they still need to address — without referencing a feature spec.
- Discovery experiments are running continuously, not just at the start of
  a project.
- The PM can explain the product vision, the strategy, and how the current
  work connects to both.
- Prototypes are being tested with real users weekly, not quarterly.
- Engineers are involved in discovery, not just delivery. They are part of
  the problem-solving, not just the building.
- The team has reference customers they interact with regularly.
- Stakeholders feel heard — their constraints are addressed — even though
  they don't get to dictate solutions.
- The team regularly kills ideas that fail validation, and no one sees that
  as failure.

## Implications for Etak

**Emphasize:**
- Frame everything in terms of the four product risks. When a user is
  exploring an opportunity, Etak should help them think about which risks
  remain unaddressed.
- Encourage problem-framing before solution-framing. If a user jumps to
  solutions, gently surface the underlying problem.
- Support the concept of discovery as continuous and parallel with delivery,
  not a phase that ends.
- Make it easy to connect current work to a product vision and strategy.

**De-emphasize:**
- Avoid language that sounds like project management or ticket tracking.
  Cagan-influenced PMs will recoil from anything that feels like Jira.
- Don't center the workflow around features, roadmaps, or delivery dates.
  Center it around problems and evidence.

**Watch out for:**
- A Cagan-influenced user will be sensitive to anything that feels like
  "feature team" thinking — e.g., if Etak organizes work around solutions
  rather than problems, or if it asks "what are you building?" before
  "what problem are you solving?"
- They will expect Etak to help them think, not just record. The tool
  should challenge weak reasoning, surface missing risks, and push back
  when assumptions are untested.
- They value speed and lightweight process in discovery — heavy forms,
  required fields, or rigid templates will feel like overhead.

**Delight:**
- A tool that naturally moves from loose problem exploration to structured
  evidence-gathering would feel like home to this person.
- Helping them articulate the "why" behind a decision in terms stakeholders
  can accept — translating between discovery language and business language
  — would be highly valued.
- Surfacing which risks are still open, without being asked, would match
  how they think.
