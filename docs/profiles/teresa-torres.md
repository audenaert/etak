# Teresa Torres

## Who They Are

Teresa Torres is a product discovery coach and the author of *Continuous
Discovery Habits* (2021). She created the Opportunity Solution Tree (OST), a
visual framework for connecting desired outcomes to the opportunities and
solutions a team is exploring. She runs Product Talk, where she publishes
extensively on discovery practice. She has coached hundreds of product teams
and is known for being both rigorous and pragmatic — she insists on structured
thinking but fights against heavyweight process. Her work is the most direct
intellectual ancestor of Etak's formal model.

## Core Philosophy

Torres believes that discovery must be continuous — not a phase, not a sprint
zero, not a quarterly planning exercise, but an ongoing habit embedded in the
weekly rhythm of every product team. Her deepest conviction is that most teams
don't fail because they lack frameworks; they fail because they don't talk to
customers often enough and don't structure what they learn.

She insists on a minimum cadence of weekly customer touchpoints — "at least
weekly" is her mantra. Not quarterly research studies, not annual surveys, but
regular lightweight conversations that keep the team grounded in real customer
reality. She views this cadence as non-negotiable.

Her second deep conviction is that decisions should be framed as "compare and
contrast" rather than "should we do this?" Binary yes/no framing leads to
confirmation bias. When you compare at least three options side by side, you
make better decisions. This applies to opportunities, solutions, and
assumptions alike.

The OST is her mechanism for making the implicit structure of discovery
explicit. It connects a clear outcome at the top to the opportunities
(customer needs/pain points/desires) the team has identified, the solutions
they're considering for each opportunity, and the assumptions underlying each
solution. It is a *thinking tool*, not a documentation artifact.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)** — the core visual artifact. A tree
  with an outcome at the root, branching into opportunities, then solutions,
  then assumption tests.
- **Opportunity** — a customer need, pain point, or desire. NOT a business
  goal or feature idea. "Opportunities live in the customer's world."
- **Desired outcome** — the measurable business result the team is trying
  to drive. Must be assigned by leadership, not chosen by the team.
- **Continuous discovery** — discovery as a weekly habit, not a project
  phase. "Discovery is not something you do before you build. It's
  something you do while you build."
- **Compare and contrast** — always consider at least three options.
  "Don't ask 'should we do this?' — ask 'which of these should we do?'"
- **Assumption testing** — identifying the riskiest assumptions behind a
  solution and designing small tests to evaluate them. Not full experiments
  — lightweight tests.
- **Story mapping assumptions** — a technique for walking through a
  solution step by step to surface hidden assumptions.
- **Interview snapshots** — one-page summaries of customer interviews that
  capture the key insights in a shareable format.
- **Weekly touchpoints** — "at least weekly" conversations with customers
  or users. The non-negotiable habit.
- **Automating access to customers** — building systems so that getting
  customer time is easy, not a multi-week scheduling ordeal.
- **"The goal is not to build more. The goal is to learn faster."**
- **Experience map** — understanding the full customer experience, not just
  the product interaction.
- **Outcome-driven roadmap** — organized by the outcomes you're driving,
  not the features you're shipping.

## Questions They Ask

1. What is the specific outcome this team has been assigned?
2. How many customers or users did you talk to this week?
3. What opportunities have you identified, and how do they relate to each
   other? Can you draw the tree?
4. Are you comparing at least three opportunities before choosing one to
   pursue?
5. For the solution you're considering — what are the riskiest assumptions?
6. What is the smallest test you could run to evaluate that assumption?
7. Are you testing assumptions, or are you testing the whole solution at
   once?
8. How did you go from interview data to these opportunities? Show me the
   synthesis.
9. Are your opportunities framed from the customer's perspective, or from
   the business's perspective?
10. Is this opportunity a customer need, a pain point, or a desire — and
    does that distinction change how you'd approach it?
11. Are you falling in love with one solution, or are you genuinely
    comparing alternatives?
12. What did you learn this week that changed your tree?
13. Is your tree a living document, or something you made once and forgot?
14. How are you deciding which branch of the tree to explore next?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

- **Discovery as a phase.** The idea that you do discovery "first" and then
  move to delivery. Torres insists they happen in parallel, continuously.
- **Not talking to customers.** Her single biggest frustration. Teams that
  say they "know" what customers want but haven't talked to one in months.
- **Binary decision-making.** Evaluating one option at a time (should we
  build this? yes/no) rather than comparing alternatives. This leads to
  anchoring bias and premature commitment.
- **Solution-first thinking.** Starting with "we should build X" instead of
  identifying the opportunity space first. Features without a clear customer
  need driving them.
- **Assumption blindness.** Not recognizing or articulating the assumptions
  behind a solution. Teams that jump from idea to implementation without
  asking "what would have to be true for this to work?"
- **Big-batch research.** Quarterly research studies that take weeks to plan
  and weeks to synthesize. By the time insights arrive, the team has moved
  on.
- **Orphaned insights.** Customer research that produces a report no one
  reads. Torres wants insights connected to action through the tree.
- **Conflating opportunities with solutions.** Saying "customers need a
  dashboard" when the real opportunity is "customers can't tell if they're
  on track." The dashboard is a solution.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team has an active, up-to-date Opportunity Solution Tree that they
  reference in weekly conversations.
- They talk to customers at least weekly — and it's not a big deal, it's
  just part of how they work.
- When asked "why are you working on this?", they can trace a path from
  their assigned outcome through an opportunity to the solution they're
  testing.
- They are comparing multiple solutions and can explain why they chose the
  current one over alternatives.
- Assumptions are explicitly identified and tested with small, fast
  experiments before the team commits to building.
- Interview snapshots are being created and shared across the team — not
  just hoarded by the PM or researcher.
- The tree evolves. New opportunities are added, old ones are deprioritized,
  solutions are killed when assumptions fail. It is alive.
- Engineers and designers are involved in discovery, not just the PM.

## Implications for Etak

**Emphasize:**
- The Opportunity Solution Tree is Etak's native structure. For a
  Torres-influenced user, Etak should feel like a natural home — the tool
  that makes the OST a living, evolving artifact rather than a static
  diagram.
- Support the incremental building of the tree through conversation. A user
  should be able to talk through what they learned in a customer interview
  and have Etak help them place it on the tree.
- Prompt for compare-and-contrast framing. When a user considers a single
  solution, nudge them to consider alternatives. When they focus on one
  opportunity, ask about adjacent ones.
- Make assumption identification and testing a first-class workflow. Help
  users move from "I think this solution will work" to "what would have
  to be true?" to "how can we test that?"

**De-emphasize:**
- Heavy upfront planning. Torres values lightweight, continuous work over
  big planning events. Etak should not feel like a planning tool.
- Solution-centric organization. Don't organize the workspace around
  features; organize it around outcomes and opportunities.

**Watch out for:**
- A Torres-influenced user will be very precise about the distinction
  between opportunities (customer-side) and solutions (product-side). If
  Etak blurs this line, they will lose trust.
- They will expect the tree to be the central artifact — if Etak treats
  it as one view among many, or as an optional visualization, it will feel
  wrong.
- They care deeply about the quality of customer evidence. Etak should help
  them track where insights came from and how fresh they are.

**Delight:**
- Etak's core design philosophy of "incremental formalism" — starting with
  loose dialogue and gradually crystallizing into structure — is exactly
  how Torres describes good discovery. This alignment should be surfaced
  and celebrated.
- Helping users notice when they've been exploring one branch of the tree
  for too long and haven't updated other branches would match her emphasis
  on breadth-first exploration.
- Automatic prompting to revisit the tree after a customer conversation
  ("you mentioned talking to a user — did anything change on your tree?")
  would feel like a good coach.
- Making it trivially easy to create interview snapshots from conversation
  would remove a friction point Torres-influenced teams often struggle
  with.
