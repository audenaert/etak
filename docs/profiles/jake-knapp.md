# Jake Knapp

> Creator of the Design Sprint at Google Ventures. Author of *Sprint*.
> Believes in structured time-boxing as the antidote to endless debate.

---

## Who They Are

Jake Knapp is a designer and author who created the Design Sprint methodology
while at Google Ventures (GV). He ran over 150 sprints with GV portfolio
companies and codified the process in *Sprint: How to Solve Big Problems and
Test New Ideas in Just Five Days* (2016), co-authored with John Zeratsky and
Braden Kowitz. He previously spent 10 years at Google, where he also created
the concept of "Design Sprints" internally.

His contribution is a specific, opinionated, highly structured process for
going from a big question to a tested prototype in five days. The sprint is
not a brainstorming session or a hackathon. It's a disciplined sequence:
understand the problem on Monday, sketch solutions on Tuesday, decide on
Wednesday, prototype on Thursday, test with real users on Friday.

Knapp matters for Etak because the Design Sprint is one of the clearest
real-world examples of **incremental formalism** — structured creativity
within constraints. The sprint takes messy, ambiguous product questions and
imposes just enough structure to produce learning without killing creativity.

## Core Philosophy

Big problems don't get solved in meetings. They get solved by individuals
thinking hard, making their thinking visible, and then converging through
structured decision-making. Knapp calls this **"together alone"** — people
work independently on the same problem, then bring their work together for
comparison and decision.

Time pressure is a feature, not a bug. Five days is not enough time to build
something real, which is exactly the point. The constraint forces the team to
focus, make decisions, and avoid the infinite polishing that kills momentum.
"Good enough to test" replaces "good enough to ship."

Prototypes are disposable thinking tools. The Thursday prototype is not a
first version of the product. It's a facade — realistic enough to get genuine
reactions from users, cheap enough to throw away without regret. The purpose
of the prototype is to learn, not to build.

Knapp deeply distrusts group brainstorming. Research shows it produces worse
ideas than individual ideation followed by structured comparison. The sprint
is designed to avoid the social dynamics that make group brainstorming
ineffective — anchoring, groupthink, loudest-voice-wins.

Decisions need a **Decider**. Someone with authority who makes the call when
the team can't reach consensus. Not a dictator — someone who listens to the
group's input and then decides. Without a Decider, sprints stall.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Design Sprint** — the five-day process: Map, Sketch, Decide, Prototype,
  Test. Not a generic term for "working fast" — a specific methodology with
  specific steps.
- **Together alone** — individual work before group convergence. Everyone
  sketches solutions independently, then the group reviews and votes. This
  avoids anchoring and groupthink.
- **The Decider** — the person with authority to make final calls. Usually the
  CEO or product lead. They participate all week but their decision power is
  invoked when the team is stuck.
- **How Might We (HMW)** — reframing problems as opportunities. "The checkout
  flow is confusing" becomes "How might we make checkout feel effortless?" Used
  during Monday mapping.
- **Lightning Demos** — rapid reviews of existing solutions (competitors,
  analogous products) for inspiration. Not to copy, but to identify patterns
  worth borrowing.
- **Crazy 8s** — a sketching exercise: fold a paper into 8 panels, sketch 8
  variations of an idea in 8 minutes. Forces rapid exploration before
  premature commitment.
- **Solution Sketch** — a detailed, three-panel storyboard of one solution
  concept. Individual work, not group brainstorming. This is what gets voted
  on.
- **Dot voting** — silent voting with stickers. Everyone reviews sketches
  silently, places dots on ideas they find compelling. Avoids influence from
  vocal advocates.
- **Storyboard** — Wednesday's output. A step-by-step plan for Thursday's
  prototype, detailed enough to build from.
- **Goldilocks quality** — the prototype should be realistic enough to test
  but no more. Not too rough (users can't engage), not too polished (team
  gets attached).
- **Five-user test** — Friday's test with 5 real users. Based on Nielsen's
  research: 5 users reveal ~85% of usability issues. You don't need more.

## Questions They Ask

1. "What's the big question we're trying to answer this week?"
2. "If this fails, what will have gone wrong?"
3. "Who's the Decider?"
4. "What do we already know? What's the existing research telling us?"
5. "How might we [reframe of the problem]?"
6. "What are we seeing in these solution sketches that's surprising?"
7. "Can we prototype this in a day? If not, what's the simplest version
   that still tests the core question?"
8. "What does the user need to believe for this to work?"
9. "Are we testing the right thing, or just the easiest thing to build?"
10. "What did we learn from the five users today? What patterns emerged?"
11. "Where did users get stuck or confused?"
12. "Is this a 'nice to know' or does it change what we do next?"
13. "Are we debating opinions or comparing evidence?"

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Death by meeting.** Endless discussions that circle without converging.
Knapp's entire methodology is a response to this. If you can't make progress
on a big question in five days of focused work, more meetings won't help.

**Group brainstorming.** The research is clear: group brainstorming produces
fewer and worse ideas than individual ideation. Knapp designs the sprint to
prevent it — people sketch alone, compare silently, vote without discussion.

**Analysis paralysis.** Teams that keep researching, discussing, and analyzing
rather than making something tangible and testing it. The sprint forces the
issue: by Thursday, you're building. By Friday, you're testing.

**Pixel-perfect prototypes.** Teams that spend too long making the prototype
beautiful. Goldilocks quality: real enough to test, rough enough to throw away.
If the team can't prototype it in a day, scope it down.

**Hippo decisions (Highest Paid Person's Opinion).** The sprint uses structured
exercises — silent voting, independent sketching — specifically to prevent the
loudest or most senior person from dominating. The Decider has authority, but
only after the group's input is visible.

**Testing with insiders.** Friday's test must be with real external users, not
colleagues or friends. Insiders have too much context and too much politeness
to give useful reactions.

**Sprinting on the wrong question.** A sprint works when there's a meaningful,
answerable question. "Should we build this?" is a sprint question. "What should
our product strategy be?" is too broad. Knapp spends Monday narrowing the focus.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team can name the specific question the sprint is answering.
- Individual ideation precedes group discussion. People sketch before they
  talk.
- There's a Decider who participates and exercises their authority when
  needed.
- The prototype is realistic enough that users engage genuinely with it —
  they don't know it's fake.
- Five users test the prototype, and the team watches together.
- After testing, the team can articulate what they learned and what they'll
  do differently.
- The sprint produces a clear next step — not a completed product, but a
  decision about direction.
- The team is willing to throw away the prototype. It served its purpose.

## Implications for Etak

**When a user thinks like Knapp, Etak should:**

- Support rapid framing and convergence. Knapp-style users want to move
  from ambiguity to a testable question quickly. Etak should help narrow
  the focus: "What's the one question we'd want to answer this week?"
- Enable structured comparison of alternatives. The opportunity solution
  tree is a natural fit — multiple solutions branching from a single
  opportunity, evaluated against each other rather than in isolation.
- Ask about evidence from users, not opinions from the team. When
  evaluating solutions, Etak should push toward "have we tested this?"
  rather than "do we like this?"
- Support time-boxing and scope discipline. When a user is expanding scope,
  Etak might ask: "Can we test the core question with something simpler?"
- Help formulate prototype-worthy questions. Not every opportunity needs a
  full sprint, but the discipline of asking "what's the fastest way to
  learn whether this works?" applies broadly.
- Distinguish between "we decided" and "we learned." Knapp cares about
  evidence-based decisions, not consensus-based ones. Etak should track
  which decisions are backed by user testing and which are assumptions.

**Risks to manage:**

- Sprint methodology is designed for a specific context — early-stage
  product questions with a cross-functional team. Not everything is a
  sprint. Etak should recognize when the user is in a different mode.
- The five-day structure doesn't translate directly to a conversational
  tool. Etak should borrow the principles (time-box, prototype, test)
  without trying to simulate the physical sprint experience.
- Knapp's approach emphasizes speed and decisiveness, which can sometimes
  short-circuit deeper strategic thinking. Etak should honor the pace
  while occasionally asking: "Are we solving the right problem?"
