# Ryan Singer

> Design reference profile for Etak product discovery adaptation.

---

## Who They Are

Ryan Singer was Head of Strategy at Basecamp (37signals) for over 17 years and
is the author of *Shape Up: Stop Running in Circles and Ship Work that Matters*
(2019, free online). He has since left Basecamp and continues to teach and
consult on the Shape Up methodology.

Singer represents a fundamentally different tradition from mainstream product
management. Where most PM thinking traces its lineage through Lean Startup,
Scrum, and SAFe, Singer's thinking comes from Basecamp's iconoclastic culture
— small teams, long cycles, no backlogs, no sprints, no estimation in the
traditional sense. His framework is a complete rejection of the Agile
industrial complex.

Key contributions:
- *Shape Up* — a full methodology for how to choose, scope, and build features
- The concept of "appetite" as a replacement for estimation
- Shaping as a senior design/strategy activity
- Breadboarding and fat marker sketches as shaping tools
- Hill charts as a progress visualization
- Scope hammering — cutting scope to fit a fixed time box

---

## Core Philosophy

Time is the fundamental constraint. Instead of estimating how long something
will take and then committing to that estimate, you decide how much time
something is *worth* (the appetite) and then shape the work to fit within that
constraint. This inverts the relationship between scope and schedule.

Discovery and delivery are not separate phases — they are interleaved within a
cycle. "Shaping" happens before a cycle begins (this is the strategic/discovery
work), but significant discovery continues during the build phase as the small
team figures out the actual solution within the shaped boundaries.

Backlogs are harmful. They create a false sense of obligation, grow without
bound, and become a guilt-inducing pile of "someday" items. Instead, ideas
should be pitched fresh each cycle. If something is important, it will come
back. If it doesn't come back, it wasn't important.

Senior people should shape; small teams should build. Shaping is a skilled
activity that requires understanding the business, the technical landscape, and
the design space. It produces a shaped pitch — not a spec, not wireframes, but
a solution sketched at the right level of abstraction. Too abstract and the
team has no direction; too concrete and you've removed their autonomy.

Building happens in 6-week cycles with full team autonomy. No daily standups,
no sprint planning, no check-ins. The team figures out scopes, sequences work,
and ships. Hill charts provide async visibility into progress without
interrupting flow.

## Discovery Vocabulary

**Signature terms and phrases:**
- "Appetite" — how much time is this problem worth? (replaces estimation)
- "Shaping" — the upstream work of roughing out a solution before betting on it
- "Shaped pitch" — a write-up that describes the problem, the appetite, the
  solution sketch, rabbit holes, and no-gos
- "Fat marker sketch" — a deliberately low-fidelity drawing that leaves room
  for the builder's judgment
- "Breadboard" — a UI flow sketch showing affordances, connections, and places
  without any visual design
- "Betting table" — where leadership decides what to bet the next cycle on
- "Cool-down" — the period between cycles for ad hoc work and recovery
- "Hill chart" — a progress visualization showing work moving from "figuring
  it out" (uphill) to "making it happen" (downhill)
- "Scope hammering" — cutting scope aggressively to fit the appetite
- "Rabbit holes" — known risks flagged in the pitch so the team avoids them
- "No-gos" — things explicitly excluded from the pitch
- "Circuit breaker" — if work isn't done when the cycle ends, it doesn't
  automatically continue. You must re-pitch it.
- "Raw ideas" — unshaped, unvetted suggestions (never commit to these)

**Characteristic phrasing:**
- "What's the appetite for this?"
- "That's a raw idea — it needs shaping before we can bet on it."
- "Is this uphill or downhill?"
- "What are the rabbit holes?"
- "We're not going to estimate this. We're going to ask: is this worth six weeks?"
- "The team has full autonomy within the shaped boundaries."
- "If it doesn't ship in the cycle, the circuit breaker trips."

**Metaphors:**
- The hill (figuring out vs. making it happen)
- The betting table (leadership as resource allocators, not taskmasters)
- Fat markers (forcing appropriate abstraction by using a thick pen)
- Circuit breakers (preventing runaway projects)

## Questions They Ask

1. What is the appetite for this problem? Is it a small batch (2 weeks) or a big batch (6 weeks)? Or is it not worth betting on at all?
2. Is this shaped, or is it still a raw idea? What's missing before we could bet on it?
3. What does the solution look like at a fat-marker level? Can you sketch it without specifying every detail?
4. What are the rabbit holes? Where could a team get stuck for days without making progress?
5. What are the no-gos? What are we explicitly *not* including, even if someone asks for it?
6. Who is this for, and what is the struggling moment that triggers the need?
7. Is this work uphill or downhill right now? Are we still figuring it out, or are we executing on a known approach?
8. What would we cut if we had to ship this in half the time?
9. Does this pitch have a clear boundary? Could a team look at this and know when they're done?
10. If we don't build this, what happens? Will the pitch come back next cycle, or will we forget about it?
11. Is this one scope or multiple scopes? Can we break this into independently shippable pieces?
12. What existing behavior are we changing? What are the switching costs for the user?
13. Have we de-risked the hardest part, or are we assuming the risky part will just work out?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Backlogs.** An ever-growing list of ideas that creates false obligation and
prevents fresh thinking. Singer wants to burn the backlog. If something
matters, it will come back.

**Estimation.** Estimating how long work will take is a fool's errand. Instead,
fix the time (appetite) and vary the scope. Estimation pretends you know
things you don't know.

**Sprints and daily standups.** Artificial ceremony that interrupts flow and
creates management overhead. Two-week sprints are too short for meaningful
work and too long for meaningful feedback.

**Premature specificity.** Wireframes and detailed specs before a team has
started building. This removes the builder's autonomy and encodes decisions
that should be made by the people closest to the work.

**Running projects.** Projects that run past their cycle without a deliberate
decision to re-bet. The circuit breaker exists to prevent sunk-cost
continuation of bad bets.

**"Just one more thing."** Scope creep within a cycle. The response is scope
hammering — cut things ruthlessly to protect the appetite.

**Fake progress.** Showing "80% done" when the hardest 20% hasn't been
figured out. Hill charts combat this by making the uphill/downhill
distinction explicit.

**Separating discovery and delivery.** Treating "research" and "building" as
sequential phases done by different people. In Shape Up, discovery continues
during the build — the team figures out the solution within the shaped
boundaries.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- Leadership spends time shaping pitches — writing up problems with
  appetites, solution sketches, rabbit holes, and no-gos. Not just listing
  feature requests.
- The betting table is a real decision point where leadership chooses what to
  bet on based on appetite and strategic fit, not a rubber stamp for a
  pre-existing roadmap.
- Teams have full autonomy during the cycle. No one is assigning tasks or
  checking in daily. Hill charts provide async visibility.
- Scope is hammered to fit the appetite. Teams routinely ship meaningful work
  within the time box by cutting the right things.
- The circuit breaker is used. Sometimes work doesn't ship, and that's a
  feature of the system, not a failure. Re-pitch it or let it go.
- Cool-down periods exist between cycles. Teams breathe, fix small things,
  and explore.
- There is no backlog. Ideas are pitched fresh. The cultural expectation is
  that most ideas won't be bet on, and that's fine.
- People at the right level of seniority are shaping. Shaping is treated as
  a first-class strategic activity, not something a junior PM does in a Jira
  ticket.

## Implications for Etak

**Appetite-first framing.** When a Singer-aligned user describes an opportunity,
Etak should immediately ask about appetite, not scope. "How much time is this
problem worth?" before "What would we build?" This inverts the usual flow.

**Shaping as the core activity.** For this user, discovery *is* shaping. Etak
should support the shaping process: helping rough out solutions at the right
level of abstraction, identifying rabbit holes, defining no-gos, and
producing something that looks like a shaped pitch.

**No backlog modeling.** Do not treat the opportunity solution tree as a backlog.
Ideas that aren't actively being shaped or bet on should fade, not accumulate.
Etak should resist the urge to "save everything." Make it easy to let go of
ideas.

**Breadboarding and fat marker support.** Etak's incremental formalism approach
aligns well here. Start with loose dialogue about the problem and the
struggling moment, then help the user crystallize into breadboard-level flows
and fat-marker-level sketches (as text descriptions, since Etak is CLI-based).

**Hill chart thinking.** When a user is working through a shaped pitch, help
them assess which parts are uphill (still being figured out) and which are
downhill (approach is clear). This is a natural checkpoint question.

**Anti-accumulation.** Singer-aligned users will resist any feature of Etak that
feels like a backlog or a tracker. Keep the tool feeling like a thinking
partner that helps shape and decide, not a system that records and accumulates.

**Cycle-aware structure.** If the user works in cycles, Etak should understand
that context. "Are you shaping for the next betting table?" vs. "Are you
building within a cycle right now?" These are different modes requiring
different support.
