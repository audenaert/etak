# Christina Wodtke

> Design reference profile for Etak product discovery adaptation.

---

## Who They Are

Christina Wodtke is an author, speaker, and lecturer at Stanford University.
She is best known for *Radical Focus: Achieving Your Most Important Goals with
Objectives and Key Results* (first edition 2016, second edition 2021), which
popularized OKRs for product teams through a narrative-driven format. She also
authored *Information Architecture: Blueprints for the Web* (2002) and
*Pencil Me In* on sketching and visual thinking.

Wodtke bridges the gap between strategy and execution. Her early career in
information architecture gave her a structural mind — how to organize
complexity so people can navigate it. Her OKR work applies that same
structural thinking to goal-setting: how do you organize ambition so teams
can navigate toward it? She is practical and implementation-focused, less
interested in theory than in how real teams actually set and pursue goals.

Key contributions:
- Popularizing OKRs for product teams via narrative (not just methodology)
- The "committed vs. aspirational" OKR distinction
- Confidence levels as a weekly check-in mechanism
- The OKR cadence — not just what you set, but the rhythm of checking in
- Early and influential work on information architecture for the web
- Teaching product management practice at Stanford

## Core Philosophy

Goals without rhythm are wishes. Setting OKRs is the easy part — the hard
part is the weekly practice of checking in, assessing confidence, and
adjusting. Most teams fail at OKRs not because they set bad objectives but
because they abandon the rhythm after two weeks.

Objectives should be qualitative and inspiring. They should describe a
future state that the team wants to reach — something that sounds like a
mission, not a metric. "Delight first-time users" is an objective.
"Increase activation rate by 15%" is a key result. The objective tells you
*where* you're going; the key results tell you *how you'll know* you arrived.

Key results must be measurable and time-bound, but they are not tasks. A
key result describes an outcome — a change in the world — not an activity.
"Launch feature X" is not a key result. "50% of new users complete onboarding
in the first session" is a key result. The distinction matters because
outcome-oriented key results allow teams to find creative paths to the goal.

Committed OKRs are promises; aspirational OKRs are bets. Committed OKRs
(confidence level 10/10) are things the team is certain they will deliver —
failing these indicates a planning or execution problem. Aspirational OKRs
(confidence level ~5/10) are stretch goals — hitting them would be remarkable.
Failing aspirational OKRs is expected and fine. Conflating these two types
causes dysfunction: teams either sandbag (treating everything as committed) or
burn out (treating everything as aspirational).

Confidence levels are the heartbeat of the system. Every week (or at whatever
cadence works), the team assesses their confidence in hitting each key result
on a scale of 1-10. A dropping confidence level is an early warning signal.
A flat confidence level despite effort means something is wrong with the
approach. Confidence tracking turns OKRs from a set-and-forget exercise into
a living instrument.

Strategy provides the "why" behind objectives. OKRs do not replace strategy
— they operationalize it. Without a clear strategy, OKRs become a collection
of disconnected goals. With a clear strategy, OKRs are the mechanism by which
strategy becomes real.

## Discovery Vocabulary

**Signature terms and phrases:**
- "Radical focus" — ruthless prioritization on the most important thing
- "Objectives" — qualitative, inspiring descriptions of a desired future state
- "Key results" — measurable outcomes that indicate the objective is being met
- "Committed vs. aspirational" — two types of OKRs with different expectations
- "Confidence level" — weekly assessment of likelihood of hitting a key result
- "Cadence" / "rhythm" — the regular practice of checking in on OKRs
- "Health metrics" — things you watch to make sure you're not breaking
  something while pursuing your objective
- "Sandbagging" — setting OKRs you know you'll hit to look good
- "Narrative" — using story structure to make goals meaningful and memorable
- "Information architecture" — organizing complexity for human navigation

**Characteristic phrasing:**
- "What's the one thing that matters most this quarter?"
- "That's a task, not a key result. What outcome would the task produce?"
- "Is that committed or aspirational? Because the answer changes everything."
- "What's your confidence level this week? Has it changed? Why?"
- "You've got seven objectives. That's not focus — that's a wish list."
- "The goal is the destination. The key results are the signposts. The tasks
  are the route. Don't confuse them."
- "If your confidence hasn't moved in three weeks, something is wrong."

**Metaphors:**
- OKRs as a compass, not a map (direction over prescription)
- Confidence levels as vital signs (monitoring health, not just checking boxes)
- The weekly cadence as a heartbeat (regular rhythm that keeps the system alive)
- Goals as stories (narrative makes objectives memorable and motivating)

## Questions They Ask

1. What is the single most important thing for this team to achieve this quarter? If you could only accomplish one thing, what would it be?
2. Is this objective inspiring? Does it describe a future state the team actually wants to reach, or is it just a restatement of a metric?
3. How will you know you've achieved this? What measurable outcome would convince you?
4. Is this key result an outcome or an activity? "Launch X" is an activity. What would launching X *change* that you could measure?
5. Is this OKR committed or aspirational? What's your confidence level right now — and what's it based on?
6. How many OKRs do you have? If it's more than three objectives, what would you cut?
7. What's the cadence for checking in on these? Weekly? Bi-weekly? If there's no cadence, these are just wall art.
8. What are your health metrics — the things you need to watch so you don't break something while chasing the objective?
9. Has your confidence level changed since last week? Why? What happened or didn't happen?
10. How do these OKRs connect to the company's strategy? Can you draw a line from your team's objective to a strategic priority?
11. What's the narrative? Can you tell me the story of why this objective matters — not in metrics, but in human terms?
12. What did you learn from last quarter's OKRs? Which ones were set well? Which were broken from the start?
13. Are you measuring leading indicators or lagging indicators? Can you find something more leading?
14. If you hit all your key results but the objective still doesn't feel achieved, what's missing from your key results?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Too many objectives.** Teams that set five, seven, ten objectives per quarter.
This is the opposite of focus. Radical focus means choosing the one or two
things that matter most and being willing to say "not this quarter" to
everything else.

**Key results as task lists.** "Launch feature X," "Complete design for Y,"
"Ship integration with Z." These are activities, not outcomes. If the key
result doesn't describe a change in the world that you can observe, it's a
task wearing an OKR costume.

**Set and forget.** Writing OKRs at the beginning of the quarter and not
looking at them again until the end. Without the weekly cadence of confidence
checking, OKRs are just a planning ritual with no operational value.

**Sandbagging.** Setting OKRs the team knows they can hit so they can report
100% completion. This defeats the purpose. Aspirational OKRs should have
~50% confidence at the outset — they're supposed to be a stretch.

**Conflating committed and aspirational.** Treating all OKRs the same.
Committed OKRs must be hit; aspirational OKRs are bets. When leadership
treats aspirational OKRs as commitments, teams learn to sandbag. When teams
treat committed OKRs as aspirational, critical work gets dropped.

**Objectives as metrics.** "Increase revenue by 20%" is not an objective —
it's a key result with no inspiring objective above it. Objectives should
describe a meaningful future state: "Become the obvious choice for mid-market
teams." Then the revenue metric is a key result beneath that.

**No connection to strategy.** OKRs that exist in a strategic vacuum. Teams
set goals based on what's in front of them rather than connecting to
broader strategic priorities. This leads to local optimization.

**Ignoring health metrics.** Chasing an objective while ignoring the damage
you're doing to other things. Increasing acquisition while destroying
retention. Shipping faster while accumulating technical debt. Health
metrics are guardrails.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- Teams have one or two objectives per quarter, rarely three, never more.
  Each one is qualitative and describes an inspiring future state.
- Key results are measurable outcomes, not activities. The team can explain
  what change in the world each key result represents.
- The distinction between committed and aspirational OKRs is clear. The team
  knows which is which, and leadership has appropriate expectations for each.
- A weekly cadence exists. Every week, the team assesses confidence levels
  for each key result. Changes in confidence trigger conversation.
- Health metrics are defined and monitored. The team knows what they're
  watching to avoid unintended damage.
- OKRs connect visibly to strategy. A team member can explain how their
  objectives support the company's strategic priorities.
- Quarterly retrospectives happen. At the end of the quarter, the team
  reviews what they learned — not just whether they hit the numbers, but
  what the numbers taught them about the business.
- Narratives exist. The team can tell the story of their objective in human
  terms — why it matters, who benefits, what the world looks like when
  it's achieved.
- OKRs evolve over time. The team gets better at setting them each quarter.
  Early OKRs are often poorly formed — that's expected. The practice
  improves with repetition.

## Implications for Etak

**Objective-outcome alignment.** For a Wodtke-aligned user, every opportunity
and solution in the tree should trace back to an objective. Etak should ask:
"Which objective does this opportunity serve?" and "What key result would
move if this solution works?" This connects discovery to the goal-setting
framework naturally.

**Outcome-oriented key results.** When a user describes a solution, Etak
should help them articulate the expected outcome in measurable terms. Not
"build X" but "what would change if X works?" This mirrors the key-result
discipline of describing outcomes, not activities.

**Confidence tracking.** Etak could integrate confidence levels into the
opportunity tree. As the user explores an opportunity, ask: "What's your
confidence that this is the right opportunity? Has it changed since you
started exploring?" This brings the weekly cadence sensibility into
discovery.

**Focus pressure.** When a Wodtke-aligned user adds many opportunities, Etak
should gently push for focus. "You have six opportunities in play. Which
one or two are most important right now? What happens if you defer the
others?" This mirrors radical focus.

**Committed vs. aspirational for opportunities.** The committed/aspirational
distinction could apply to the opportunity tree. Some opportunities are
must-pursue (high confidence, strategic necessity); others are exploratory
bets (lower confidence, higher potential upside). Etak could help the user
classify and treat them differently.

**Cadence awareness.** This user thinks in rhythms — quarterly objectives,
weekly check-ins. Etak should be aware of these cadences. "You're three
weeks into the quarter — how does this discovery work connect to your
current OKRs?" Temporal context matters.

**Incremental formalism alignment.** Good alignment. Wodtke's narrative
approach to OKRs — start with a story about why the objective matters,
then gradually formalize into measurable key results — mirrors Etak's
philosophy of starting loose and crystallizing. Help the user tell the
story first, then structure it.

**Health metrics as guardrails.** When the user explores aggressive
opportunities, Etak should ask about health metrics. "If you pursue this,
what could break? What would you want to watch to make sure you're not
causing unintended damage?"
