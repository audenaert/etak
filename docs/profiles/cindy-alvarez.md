# Cindy Alvarez

> Author of *Lean Customer Development*. Former Microsoft and Yammer.
> The practitioner's guide to actually learning from customers.

---

## Who They Are

Cindy Alvarez is a product leader and researcher who wrote *Lean Customer
Development: Building Products Your Customers Will Buy* (2014). She has held
product and research roles at Microsoft, Yammer (pre- and post-acquisition), and
several startups. She currently works in product leadership at a major tech
company.

Her contribution is unusually practical. Where others talk about "talking to
customers" as a principle, Alvarez wrote the manual on how to actually do it.
How to recruit interviewees. What to say in the first 30 seconds. How to ask
follow-up questions without leading. How to interpret the gap between what
people say and what they do. How to do useful research when you have no budget
and no time.

She is the person you read when you've nodded along to "get out of the building"
but don't know what to do once you're outside.

## Core Philosophy

You cannot build a good product without understanding your customer, and you
cannot understand your customer without talking to them. But talking to customers
is a skill, not a personality trait, and most people do it badly.

The core problem is that customers are unreliable narrators of their own behavior.
They tell you what they think you want to hear. They describe aspirational behavior,
not actual behavior. They generalize from recent experience. They can't predict
their own future actions. None of this is dishonesty — it's just how human memory
and social interaction work.

Good customer development is **hypothesis-driven**. You don't go into a conversation
to "learn about the customer." You go in with a specific belief you're trying to
validate or invalidate. The conversation is an experiment, not an exploration.

Alvarez believes in **minimum viable research**. You don't need a research team or
a six-week study. Five conversations, done well, will teach you more than a
500-person survey done poorly. The barrier to customer learning is not resources —
it's willingness to be wrong.

She also emphasizes that customer development is continuous, not a phase. You don't
do it once at the start and then build. You keep doing it, at every stage.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Customer development** — the practice of systematically learning from customers
  to reduce risk. Not the same as user research (broader) or sales (different goal).
- **Earlyvangelists** — Steve Blank's term that Alvarez operationalizes. These are
  customers who have the problem, know they have it, are actively looking for a
  solution, and have budget or authority to act. They're your first interview
  targets and your first customers.
- **Say vs. do gap** — the fundamental challenge. What customers tell you in an
  interview is not what they actually do. Design your research to account for this.
- **Hypothesis-driven interviews** — every interview should test a specific belief.
  "We believe [target customer] struggles with [problem] when [context]." The
  interview is designed to confirm or refute that belief.
- **Minimum viable research** — the smallest amount of research that reduces your
  biggest risk. Don't gold-plate research any more than you'd gold-plate a product.
- **Leading questions** — the interviewer's worst enemy. "Would you use a feature
  that does X?" is not research. It's confirmation bias with a question mark.
- **Behavioral questions** — questions about what someone actually did, not what
  they would do. "Tell me about the last time you..." is the most powerful opener.
- **Problem interview vs. solution interview** — different conversations with
  different goals. Don't mix them.
- **Invalidation** — the most valuable outcome of customer development. Learning
  that your hypothesis is wrong saves you months of building the wrong thing.

## Questions They Ask

1. "Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem area]. Walk me through
   what happened."
2. "What did you do next? And then what?"
3. "You said that was frustrating — what made it frustrating specifically?"
4. "What are you doing today to solve this? What tools or workarounds?"
5. "What's the hardest part of that process for you?"
6. "Have you looked for a better solution? What happened when you tried?"
7. "Who else is involved when this comes up? What's their role?"
8. "When you say 'often,' how often do you mean? Once a week? Once a day?"
9. "What would happen if you just didn't do this at all?"
10. "You mentioned [thing]. Can you give me a specific example?"
11. "What hypothesis are we testing with this interview?"
12. "How many customers have we talked to, and what pattern are we seeing?"
13. "Are we hearing what they actually do, or what they wish they did?"
14. "Is this a problem they're actively trying to solve, or one they've
    accepted?"

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Asking leading questions.** "Wouldn't it be great if you could..." is not
customer development. It's a sales pitch disguised as a question. Alvarez is
relentless about this. The moment you describe your solution, the interview is
contaminated.

**Taking customer requests at face value.** A customer says "I need a report."
They don't need a report. They need information that reduces uncertainty so they
can make a decision. The request is a solution. Your job is to find the need
underneath it.

**Talking to the wrong people.** Interviewing people who are polite but have no
urgency around the problem. You need earlyvangelists — people who feel the pain
and are motivated to solve it. If your interviewee shrugs and says "yeah, that's
kind of annoying I guess," you're talking to the wrong person.

**Surveys as a substitute for conversations.** Surveys can quantify a pattern
you've already identified. They cannot discover problems you don't know about.
Alvarez sees teams use surveys to avoid the discomfort of direct conversation.

**Confirmation bias.** Hearing what you want to hear. Remembering the one
enthusiastic customer and forgetting the four who were indifferent. Alvarez
pushes for structured note-taking and pattern recognition across interviews,
not cherry-picking quotes.

**Waiting for perfect research.** Teams that won't make a decision until they
have statistical significance from a large sample. Customer development is
qualitative and directional. Five good interviews beat fifty bad survey
responses.

**Doing research once.** Treating customer conversations as a phase that
happens before "real work" begins. Alvarez insists that learning from
customers is continuous — you should be talking to customers every week,
not once a quarter.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team has a running list of hypotheses they're testing, and interviews
  are designed around those hypotheses.
- Interview notes distinguish between direct quotes, observed behavior, and
  the interviewer's interpretation.
- The team can point to specific things they *stopped* believing because of
  customer conversations.
- Research findings are shared quickly and informally — not hoarded in a
  research report that no one reads.
- Questions in interviews are open-ended and behavioral. "Tell me about the
  last time..." appears frequently in interview guides.
- The team is talking to customers every week, not just during a "research
  phase."
- When a stakeholder says "customers want X," someone asks "which customers,
  and how do we know?"

## Implications for Etak

**When a user thinks like Alvarez, Etak should:**

- Help formulate hypotheses before interviews. When a user is exploring an
  opportunity, Etak should ask: "What would we need to believe for this
  opportunity to be real? How would we test that?"
- Support interview planning. Help draft interview questions that are
  behavioral and open-ended. Flag leading questions when the user drafts them.
- Track what's been learned from customers as a first-class element of
  the opportunity tree. Not just "we identified this opportunity" but
  "here's the evidence — 4 of 6 customers described this pain."
- Distinguish between what customers said and what the team concludes. Etak
  should support a layer of interpretation between raw notes and conclusions.
- Nudge toward invalidation. When a user is building a case for an
  opportunity, Etak might ask: "What would make us abandon this? Have we
  looked for disconfirming evidence?"
- Support lightweight, ongoing research. Not big formal studies, but a
  rhythm of small conversations. Etak could track "when did we last talk
  to a customer about this?"

**Risks to manage:**

- Alvarez-style thinking can lead to analysis paralysis in teams that use
  "we need more research" as a reason to defer decisions. Etak should
  recognize when enough evidence exists to move forward.
- Not every question needs a customer interview. Sometimes the team has
  enough internal knowledge or data to make a call. Etak shouldn't push
  research for its own sake.
