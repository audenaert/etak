# Jeff Patton

## Who They Are

Jeff Patton is the author of *User Story Mapping* (2014) and a long-time agile
practitioner and coach. He has been working in iterative and agile software
development since the early 1990s and was one of the original signatories
thinking about how user stories actually work in practice. He is most famous
for the story mapping technique, which provides a visual, spatial way to
organize user stories into a coherent narrative of the user's experience. He
has been influenced by and collaborated with Jeff Gothelf (Lean UX) and has
deep roots in the interaction design and UX communities. He is a practitioner
first — someone who has spent decades facilitating rooms full of people trying
to build shared understanding.

## Core Philosophy

Patton's deepest conviction is that **shared understanding is the real
deliverable of product planning** — not the documents, not the stories, not
the map itself. "The card is a token for a conversation," he insists. The
physical or visual artifact is never the point; the point is the understanding
that forms in people's heads when they collaborate to create it.

He believes that most product planning fails because teams confuse
documentation with communication. Writing detailed requirements does not create
understanding. Handing someone a spec does not transfer knowledge. The only
reliable way to build shared understanding is through *collaborative, visual,
conversational work* — people standing together, arranging things spatially,
arguing, and aligning.

This leads to a second conviction: **plans should be visual and spatial, not
linear.** A flat backlog is a terrible thinking tool because it strips away
narrative and context. A story map preserves the "big picture" — the user's
journey through the experience — while also showing the details. You can see
the forest and the trees at the same time.

Patton is suspicious of any process that separates thinking from doing, or
planning from building. He comes from a Lean UX sensibility where the team
learns by building and builds to learn.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Story map** — a two-dimensional arrangement of user stories. The
  horizontal axis is the narrative flow of the user's experience (the
  "backbone"). The vertical axis is detail and variation, organized by
  priority. "The map is the conversation."
- **The backbone** — the top row of the story map, representing the high-
  level activities or steps in the user's journey.
- **Walking skeleton** — the minimum slice through the story map that
  creates a functional, end-to-end experience. Not an MVP — a skeleton
  you can build on.
- **"The card is a token for a conversation."** — user stories are
  reminders to have a discussion, not specifications. If you haven't had
  the conversation, the card is meaningless.
- **Shared understanding** — the state where everyone on the team has a
  sufficiently aligned mental model of what they're building and why.
  This is the actual goal of planning.
- **Output, outcome, impact** — Patton uses this hierarchy. Output is what
  you release. Outcome is the change in user behavior. Impact is the
  business result.
- **"Build less"** — a recurring theme. The purpose of mapping is partly
  to see what you can cut. "The most important thing about story mapping
  is that it helps you build less."
- **Minimum viable product solution** — Patton prefers to talk about
  "minimum viable product solutions" to avoid the confusion around MVP.
- **Externalize your thinking** — get ideas out of your head and into a
  shared visual space where others can see, react, and build on them.
- **"Think, make, check"** — a simplified design thinking loop.
- **Dual-track development** — running discovery and delivery in parallel.
  Patton has used this term since before it was widely adopted.

## Questions They Ask

1. Can you walk me through the user's experience from beginning to end?
   Tell me the story.
2. If you put this on a wall, what would it look like? How would you
   arrange it spatially?
3. Does everyone on the team have the same understanding of what we're
   building, or are we assuming alignment that doesn't exist?
4. What is the thinnest slice through this experience that would be
   functional end-to-end?
5. Have you had the conversation this story represents, or is this card
   just a placeholder?
6. What can we cut? What's in here that isn't essential to the first
   release?
7. Who is the user, and what were they doing before they encountered our
   product? What happens after?
8. If I asked each person on this team to describe what we're building,
   would I get the same answer?
9. Are we documenting or communicating? Because those are different things.
10. What is the outcome we're hoping to see — not what we're building, but
    what changes in the user's life?
11. Can you show me the big picture? I don't want to start with details.
12. When was the last time the whole team was in a room together talking
    about this?
13. Are we building this to learn, or building this to ship?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

- **Flat backlogs.** A prioritized list of stories with no narrative
  structure, no spatial organization, no sense of the whole. Patton
  considers this a fundamental planning failure.
- **Documentation as substitute for conversation.** Detailed specs,
  acceptance criteria, and PRDs that no one discusses. "If shared
  understanding was the goal, we'd stop writing and start talking."
- **Premature detail.** Going deep on individual stories before
  understanding the whole map. You need the forest before the trees.
- **Building too much.** Teams that include everything in v1 because they
  never did the exercise of visualizing the whole and deciding what to
  cut.
- **Handoffs.** One person writes the requirements, another designs, a
  third builds. Each handoff degrades understanding. Patton wants the
  whole team involved from the start.
- **"The user story is the requirement."** Treating the three-sentence
  card as if it contains everything the developer needs to know. It
  doesn't. It's an invitation to talk.
- **Ignoring the user's broader context.** Building features without
  understanding the user's full journey — what happens before and after
  they touch your product.
- **Remote-unfriendly planning.** (A tension Patton has grappled with.)
  He deeply values physical, collaborative, spatial planning, and he
  worries about digital tools that reduce collaboration to asynchronous
  text.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team has a visible, shared map of the user's experience that
  everyone references and understands.
- Planning sessions are collaborative and visual — people are arranging
  things, arguing about sequence, finding gaps.
- The team can tell the story of the user's experience fluently, without
  looking at a document.
- Release planning is done by drawing horizontal lines across the map,
  defining thin slices, not by estimating and stacking stories.
- Conversations happen around stories before development starts. Engineers,
  designers, and PMs discuss together.
- The team regularly revisits the map, updates it, and uses it to orient
  new team members.
- There is a clear walking skeleton identified, and the team ships it
  early to learn.
- The map makes it obvious what was cut and why — the "not now" is
  visible, not forgotten.

## Implications for Etak

**Emphasize:**
- The narrative, spatial quality of product thinking. Etak should help
  users think about the user's journey as a *story*, not a list of
  features. Even in a text-based interface, the sense of narrative flow
  and spatial structure matters.
- Collaborative understanding over individual documentation. Etak should
  feel like a tool that helps you think *with* others, not a tool that
  records your solo thinking.
- Slicing and scoping. Help users identify the thinnest viable slice of
  an opportunity — the walking skeleton — before getting into details.
- The "whole before the parts" principle. Etak should encourage users to
  map the big picture before drilling into any single piece.

**De-emphasize:**
- Formal documentation. Patton-influenced users will not want Etak to
  produce specs, PRDs, or detailed requirement documents. They want
  thinking tools, not writing tools.
- Linear, list-based organization. If Etak's primary view is a flat list
  of items, it will feel wrong to this person.

**Watch out for:**
- A Patton-influenced user may be skeptical of a text-based tool for
  spatial, visual thinking. Etak needs to demonstrate that it can support
  this kind of thinking even without a whiteboard. The incremental
  formalism approach — starting with conversation and gradually building
  structure — is actually well-suited to this, but Etak should
  acknowledge the tension.
- They will care about whether the *team* is involved. A tool that only
  the PM uses in isolation would concern them. Etak should make it easy
  to share context and invite collaboration.
- They will resist any workflow that separates thinking from building.
  If Etak creates a sharp boundary between "discovery mode" and
  "delivery mode," it will feel artificial.

**Delight:**
- Helping users tell the story of their user's experience as a narrative
  that the whole team can understand — this is Patton's core value.
- Supporting the "externalize your thinking" principle by helping users
  get fuzzy ideas out of their head and into a structure that others can
  see and react to.
- Making it natural to identify what to cut. Most tools focus on what to
  add; Patton values the discipline of subtraction.
- The conversational, incremental nature of Etak's interaction model is
  actually very aligned with "the card is a token for a conversation" —
  the tool IS the conversation, and the structure emerges from it.
