# Melissa Perri

## Who They Are

Melissa Perri is the CEO of Produx Labs, a product management consultancy, and
the author of *Escaping the Build Trap* (2018). She also founded the Product
Institute, an online school for product leaders. Before consulting, she held
product roles at various startups and agencies. She is known for thinking about
product management as an *organizational* problem — not just a team-level
practice. Where others coach individual PMs or teams, Perri focuses on why
entire companies fail at product management and what structural changes are
needed to fix it. She teaches at Harvard Business School.

## Core Philosophy

Perri's deepest conviction is that most companies are stuck in "the build
trap" — they measure success by how many features they ship, not by whether
those features create value. This is not a team-level problem that can be fixed
by teaching PMs better frameworks. It is a systemic, organizational failure that
involves incentive structures, communication patterns, strategic clarity, and
the relationship between product, engineering, and the executive layer.

She believes that good product management requires organizational alignment from
the top. If the company's strategy is unclear, if success is measured by output,
if PMs are rewarded for shipping rather than learning, then no amount of
discovery practice at the team level will save you. The system will override
individual effort.

Her framework, the Product Kata, emphasizes an iterative loop: understand the
strategic direction, explore the problem, explore solutions, then optimize.
This is not a linear process but a cycle that repeats. The Kata is deliberately
borrowed from lean manufacturing — she sees product management as a discipline
that must be practiced deliberately, not a talent you're born with.

She is pragmatic and systems-oriented. She does not romanticize the PM role;
she focuses on making it function within real organizations with real
constraints.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **The Build Trap** — the organizational dysfunction of measuring success by
  shipping features. "When companies stop asking 'is this the right thing to
  build?' and start asking 'how fast can we build it?', they're in the build
  trap."
- **Product Kata** — a four-step learning cycle: understand the direction,
  problem exploration, solution exploration, solution optimization. Inspired
  by Toyota Kata.
- **Strategic gaps** — the difference between where the company is and where
  it needs to be. Strategy should be about closing gaps, not listing
  features.
- **Product-led organization** — a company that organizes around outcomes
  and gives product teams the autonomy and context to make good decisions.
- **Outputs vs. outcomes** — outputs are what you ship; outcomes are the
  changes in customer behavior or business metrics that result. The build
  trap is an output obsession.
- **Experiment to learn, not to ship** — the purpose of experiments in
  discovery is learning, not building a deliverable.
- **Communication cadence** — how strategy flows from the executive level
  to teams and how learning flows back up. Perri cares a lot about this
  bidirectional flow.
- **Product metrics** — not vanity metrics, but metrics that connect team
  activity to business outcomes. "If you can't measure whether your work
  matters, you're in the build trap."
- **Solution exploration** — distinct from problem exploration. You don't
  jump from a problem to *the* solution; you explore a space of possible
  solutions.
- **Feature factory** — a team or organization whose primary output is
  features, disconnected from strategic intent.

## Questions They Ask

1. How does the company define success for product teams — is it output
   (features shipped) or outcomes (metrics moved)?
2. Can this PM articulate the company's product strategy, or just their
   team's backlog?
3. What is the strategic gap this work is trying to close?
4. Is there a clear line from executive strategy to team-level goals, or is
   each team guessing about what matters?
5. When a product initiative fails to move the target metric, what happens?
   Does anyone notice? Does anything change?
6. How do insights from team-level discovery flow back to inform strategy?
7. Are PMs spending their time on problem exploration and solution
   exploration, or are they spending it on stakeholder management and
   ticket writing?
8. Is the organization structured around value streams, or around
   technology components?
9. What does the PM career ladder look like here — is there a path for
   PMs that doesn't require becoming a people manager?
10. How does the company decide what not to build? What is the kill
    mechanism for bad ideas?
11. Are roadmaps used as learning plans or as delivery commitments?
12. Is this team iterating based on evidence, or are they on a feature
    death march?
13. What does the executive team believe product management *is*?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

- **The Build Trap itself.** The entire book is about this. Companies that
  equate progress with shipping and never ask whether what they shipped
  mattered.
- **Lack of strategic context.** Teams that operate without understanding
  why their work matters to the company. PMs who can't explain the strategy
  because no one has communicated it to them.
- **PM as waiter.** The PM who takes orders from stakeholders and passes
  them to engineering. No ownership, no discovery, no judgment — just
  relay.
- **Vanity metrics.** Measuring things that go up and to the right but
  don't connect to business value. Page views, feature adoption percentages
  without retention data, etc.
- **Reorganizing instead of changing.** Companies that think they can fix
  product dysfunction by restructuring teams rather than changing how
  decisions are made and how success is measured.
- **"We don't have time for discovery."** The excuse that the roadmap is
  too full for learning. Perri sees this as a symptom: the roadmap is too
  full *because* there's no discovery filtering out bad ideas.
- **Scaling frameworks as a substitute for strategy.** SAFe, LeSS, or
  similar frameworks that provide process but not strategic clarity. You
  can scale a broken process perfectly.
- **Separate "innovation" teams.** Isolating discovery into a special group
  while the rest of the org stays in the build trap. This never works
  because the org rejects what the innovation team produces.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- Strategy is clearly communicated from the top and connects to measurable
  outcomes at every level of the organization.
- Product teams are organized around value streams and customer problems,
  not technology layers.
- PMs spend meaningful time on problem and solution exploration, not just
  backlog grooming and sprint planning.
- There is a regular cadence for reviewing whether initiatives are moving
  target metrics — and teams change course when they're not.
- Roadmaps communicate strategic intent and current hypotheses, not
  delivery promises.
- The organization has a healthy kill rate — ideas that don't validate
  are dropped without blame.
- Learning flows upward. When teams discover something that challenges
  the strategy, there's a mechanism for that insight to reach leadership.
- PM career growth is supported with coaching, not just promotion tracks.

## Implications for Etak

**Emphasize:**
- Help users connect their discovery work to strategic context. Etak should
  encourage users to articulate *why* this problem matters to the business,
  not just why it matters to the customer.
- Support the Product Kata loop. Etak should help users move through
  direction-setting, problem exploration, solution exploration, and
  optimization — and recognize which phase they're in.
- Surface the relationship between team-level work and organizational
  outcomes. A Perri-influenced user thinks in systems, and Etak should
  help them see the system.
- Help users distinguish between output and outcome language in their own
  thinking. If they're framing goals as "launch X," prompt them to also
  articulate what metric that should move.

**De-emphasize:**
- Pure customer-centricity without business context. Perri cares about
  customers, but she frames everything through organizational strategy.
  Etak should not feel like a customer research tool; it should feel like
  a strategic thinking tool.
- Heavyweight process. Perri is pragmatic. She wants just enough structure
  to learn, not a documentation exercise.

**Watch out for:**
- A Perri-influenced user is likely to be frustrated by tools that operate
  only at the team level. They think about organizational systems, and
  they'll want Etak to help them reason about how their work fits into the
  bigger picture.
- They may be coming from an organization that IS in the build trap. Etak
  should be sympathetic to this — not assuming an ideal environment but
  helping users navigate the real one.
- They will be skeptical of any tool that feels like it could become part
  of the build trap — e.g., if Etak starts tracking "how many discoveries
  completed" as a metric.

**Delight:**
- Helping users frame their discoveries in language that can influence
  upward — translating team-level learning into strategic arguments for
  leadership.
- Supporting the iterative nature of the Product Kata — making it natural
  to cycle back from solution exploration to problem exploration when
  evidence warrants it.
- Recognizing when a user is describing a systemic problem ("my
  stakeholders keep overriding my prioritization") and responding at the
  system level, not just the tactic level.
- Making it easy to answer "what did we learn?" at a portfolio level, not
  just a team level.
