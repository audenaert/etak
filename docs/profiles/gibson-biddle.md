# Gibson Biddle

## Who They Are

Gibson Biddle (known as "Gib") is the former VP of Product at Netflix (2005-2010)
and former CPO at Chegg. He now teaches product strategy at business schools and
through his newsletter and speaking. He is best known for the DHM (Delight,
Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing) framework for evaluating product strategy, and
for teaching through detailed Netflix case studies — how Netflix made specific
strategic decisions about personalization, original content, pricing, and
international expansion. He bridges the gap between high-level strategy and
ground-level experimentation, insisting that strategy must be hypothesis-driven
and tested through rapid experimentation, particularly A/B testing.

## Core Philosophy

Biddle's deepest conviction is that **product strategy is a set of hypotheses,
not a plan.** A strategy is not something you write in a document and execute;
it is something you hypothesize and test. The best product leaders generate
bold strategic hypotheses and then run rigorous experiments to validate or
invalidate them. Strategy and experimentation are not separate activities —
they are the same activity at different altitudes.

He evaluates strategic ideas through three lenses: Does this delight customers
in a way that is hard to copy and that enhances margin? All three must be true
for a strategy to be durable. "Delight" alone is not enough if a competitor can
replicate it next quarter. "Hard-to-copy" alone is not enough if it doesn't
make customers love the product. And none of it matters if it destroys the
business model.

He is deeply influenced by Netflix's culture of experimentation and consumer
science. At Netflix, every significant product decision was informed by A/B
tests. This was not lightweight discovery; it was industrial-scale
experimentation infrastructure. Biddle believes this discipline — hypothesis,
test, measure, learn — is what separates great product organizations from
those that rely on gut instinct or HiPPO decisions.

He is also a systems thinker who emphasizes the "lock-up" between strategy,
metrics, and tactics. These three must be mutually reinforcing: strategy tells
you where to go, metrics tell you whether you're getting there, and tactics
are the specific experiments and initiatives you run.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **DHM framework** — Delight customers in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing
  ways. The three-part test for durable product strategy.
- **Strategy/Metrics/Tactics lock-up** — the alignment between what you're
  trying to achieve (strategy), how you measure progress (metrics), and
  what you're actually doing (tactics). "If these three aren't locked up,
  you're fooling yourself."
- **Consumer science** — the discipline of running rigorous experiments to
  understand customer behavior. Not qualitative research alone, but
  systematic hypothesis testing.
- **Proxy metrics** — measurable signals that stand in for hard-to-measure
  strategic outcomes. Biddle is careful about choosing proxies that
  genuinely predict the thing you care about.
- **"Gib's Aha moments"** — the specific moments when a hypothesis is
  confirmed or overturned by experimental evidence. He structures his
  teaching around these revelatory moments.
- **High-level strategy / low-level tactics** — Biddle moves fluidly
  between altitude levels. Strategy is the "what" and "why"; tactics are
  the "how."
- **A/B testing culture** — not just running tests, but building an
  organizational culture where experimentation is the default way
  decisions are made.
- **Swimlane** — a strategic theme or pillar (e.g., personalization,
  original content) that organizes multiple related experiments and
  initiatives.
- **Rolling strategy** — strategy is not annual; it evolves as you learn.
  Each quarter's experiments inform next quarter's hypotheses.
- **Engage, retain, monetize** — a simplified model of customer lifecycle
  that Biddle uses to ground strategic discussions.
- **Product strategy = plan to operationalize your vision.**

## Questions They Ask

1. Does this initiative delight customers? How specifically?
2. Is this delight hard to copy? What makes it defensible?
3. Does this enhance or erode our margin? What's the economic model?
4. What is the hypothesis, exactly? State it as "we believe that [X] will
   result in [Y], as measured by [Z]."
5. How will we test this hypothesis? What's the experiment design?
6. What is the proxy metric, and are we confident it predicts the outcome
   we actually care about?
7. Are strategy, metrics, and tactics locked up — or are we measuring
   something different from what we're trying to achieve?
8. What swimlane does this initiative belong to? How does it fit into our
   strategic themes?
9. What did we learn from the last round of experiments, and how did it
   change our strategy?
10. Can we A/B test this, or do we need a different experimental approach?
11. Is this a "one-way door" or a "two-way door" decision? How reversible
    is it?
12. What is the customer's "aha moment" with this feature? When do they
    feel the delight?
13. Are we running enough experiments? What's our experimentation velocity?
14. If a competitor copied this tomorrow, would we still have an advantage?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

- **Strategy without testing.** Writing a strategy document and executing
  it without ever validating the hypotheses embedded in it. Strategy-as-
  faith rather than strategy-as-science.
- **Gut-driven decisions at scale.** Small companies can rely on intuition;
  scaled organizations need experimental discipline. Biddle pushes back
  on HiPPO culture — the highest-paid person's opinion winning by default.
- **Vanity metrics.** Metrics that make you feel good but don't predict
  business outcomes. Biddle insists on proxy metrics that are rigorously
  connected to what matters.
- **Strategy/tactics disconnect.** Teams running experiments that don't
  connect to strategic hypotheses, or having a strategy that no one can
  translate into testable initiatives.
- **One-dimensional strategy.** Pursuing delight without considering
  defensibility or economics. Building features customers love that
  competitors can trivially replicate or that destroy your margin.
- **Annual strategy cycles.** Setting strategy once a year and not
  updating it as experimental evidence comes in. Strategy should be
  rolling, not fixed.
- **Testing without learning.** Running A/B tests mechanically without
  synthesizing the results into strategic insight. The "aha" is the
  point, not the p-value.
- **Fear of bold hypotheses.** Playing it safe with incremental
  improvements when the strategic question demands a bold bet. Biddle
  admires Netflix's willingness to make big strategic moves (original
  content, international expansion) and test aggressively.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team can articulate their product strategy as a set of testable
  hypotheses, not a list of features or initiatives.
- DHM is applied thoughtfully — every significant initiative is evaluated
  for delight, defensibility, and economic sustainability.
- There is a clear lock-up between strategic themes (swimlanes), the
  metrics used to evaluate progress, and the experiments being run.
- Experimentation velocity is high. The team is running multiple tests
  concurrently and learning fast.
- Results from experiments feed back into strategy. The strategy evolves
  based on evidence, not just executive whim.
- Proxy metrics are chosen carefully, validated periodically, and
  abandoned when they stop predicting real outcomes.
- The team can tell the story of how their strategy has evolved over the
  last 6-12 months based on what they've learned.
- Bold strategic hypotheses are tested, not just incremental improvements.

## Implications for Etak

**Emphasize:**
- The hypothesis-driven nature of strategy and discovery. Etak should help
  users frame their ideas as hypotheses with clear expected outcomes and
  measurable signals. "We believe X will result in Y, measured by Z"
  should feel like a natural output.
- The DHM lens. When a user is evaluating an opportunity or solution, Etak
  can prompt them to consider delight, defensibility, and margin impact.
  This is a simple but powerful forcing function.
- Strategy/metrics/tactics alignment. Help users check whether their
  current work connects to their strategic hypotheses and whether their
  metrics actually measure what they claim to measure.
- Learning loops. Make it easy to record experimental results and trace
  how they change strategic thinking over time.

**De-emphasize:**
- Purely qualitative discovery. Biddle values qualitative insight but
  insists on quantitative validation. Etak should not feel like a tool
  only for interview-based discovery; it should also support the
  formulation and tracking of experiments.
- Small-bore thinking. Biddle thinks at the strategic level. Etak should
  help users zoom out from individual features to strategic themes and
  swimlanes.

**Watch out for:**
- A Biddle-influenced user will want to connect discovery to measurable
  outcomes very quickly. If Etak keeps the conversation in the qualitative
  exploration phase too long without helping frame testable hypotheses,
  they will get impatient.
- They will expect Etak to help them think about defensibility and
  economics, not just customer value. A tool that focuses exclusively on
  "what do customers want?" without considering "can we sustain this?"
  will feel incomplete.
- They may have access to or want to reference A/B test data. Etak should
  be comfortable incorporating quantitative evidence into the discovery
  narrative, not just interview quotes.
- Netflix case studies are core to how Biddle teaches. A user steeped in
  his thinking may reference Netflix patterns; Etak should understand
  these references.

**Delight:**
- Helping users evaluate opportunities through the DHM lens automatically
  — prompting for defensibility and margin impact when the user has only
  discussed customer delight.
- Supporting the formulation of crisp hypotheses. If a user describes a
  vague intuition, helping them sharpen it into "we believe X will cause
  Y, measured by Z" would match how Biddle thinks.
- Making the strategy-to-experiment connection visible. If Etak can show
  how a specific experiment ties to a strategic swimlane and how the
  results update the strategy, that would be powerful.
- Helping users build a "rolling strategy" narrative — the evolving story
  of what they've learned and how it has changed their direction over
  time. Biddle teaches through narrative, and a tool that helps users
  build their own strategic narrative would resonate deeply.
