## Who They Are

Lenny Rachitsky is a former PM at Airbnb who became the most widely read voice
in product management through his newsletter (Lenny's Newsletter, 700k+
subscribers) and podcast (Lenny's Podcast). At Airbnb, he worked on supply
growth — the host side of the marketplace — and was deeply involved in the
company's scaling from growth-stage to IPO.

His contribution is not a single framework or book. It's something harder to
replicate: he systematically interviews the best product thinkers in the world,
distills their patterns, and shares practical, data-backed frameworks that
working PMs can apply immediately. He's a practitioner who became a synthesizer
and amplifier.

Key published work includes benchmark data on retention curves, frameworks for
measuring product-market fit, analysis of growth models, and tactical guides on
topics from pricing to prioritization. He doesn't invent from scratch — he
finds what works, pressure-tests it with data and expert input, and makes it
accessible.

He matters for Etak because he represents how most practicing PMs actually
learn: not from a single guru or book, but by absorbing practical wisdom from
many sources and adapting it to their context.

## Core Philosophy

Product management is a craft, not a science, and it's learned through pattern
recognition across many examples. No single framework captures the full picture.
The best PMs are eclectic — they pull from Teresa Torres, from Marty Cagan, from
growth engineering, from behavioral economics, from whatever works.

Rachitsky is deeply **practical and data-informed**. He distrusts pure theory.
When he shares a framework, he backs it with examples from named companies and
specific metrics. "Here's how Figma measures retention." "Here's Notion's growth
loop." "Here's what good looks like, with numbers."

He believes in **product-market fit as the central question**, but operationalizes
it more concretely than most. His PMF survey framework (adapted from Sean Ellis)
gives teams a specific, measurable signal: the percentage of users who say they'd
be "very disappointed" if they could no longer use the product. He provides
benchmarks: 40%+ suggests PMF.

Growth and discovery are not separate disciplines in his world. Understanding why
users retain (or don't) is both a growth question and a discovery question. The
best product work happens when these perspectives are integrated.

He values **community and shared learning**. Product management is often lonely —
one PM on a team, no peers to compare notes with. Rachitsky's community creates
the peer learning that most PM orgs fail to provide.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **PMF survey / "very disappointed" test** — "How would you feel if you could
  no longer use this product?" Percentage answering "very disappointed" is the
  PMF signal. 40%+ is the benchmark.
- **Retention curves** — the shape of the retention curve tells you whether you
  have PMF. A curve that flattens (even at a low number) is better than one
  that trends to zero. He publishes industry benchmarks.
- **Growth loops** — sustainable growth mechanisms where the output of one
  cycle feeds the input of the next. Viral loops, content loops, paid loops.
  Not "growth hacks" — structural growth models.
- **ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)** — who is your product *really* for? Not the
  broadest possible audience, but the specific segment where you have the
  strongest fit. Narrow the ICP, deepen the fit.
- **North star metric** — the single metric that best captures the value your
  product delivers to customers. Not revenue (that's your value), but customer
  value delivered.
- **Leading indicators** — metrics that predict future outcomes. Activation
  metrics (did the user do the key action?), engagement metrics (are they
  coming back?), satisfaction metrics.
- **Benchmarks** — what "good" looks like, with numbers. Rachitsky collects
  and publishes these obsessively. Day-1 retention, day-7, day-30, by category.
  NPS by industry. Conversion rates by channel.
- **Jobs to be done** — Rachitsky references JTBD frequently as a lens for
  understanding why customers switch to (or away from) your product.
- **The "magic moment"** — the experience in a product where the user first
  gets real value. Facebook's "7 friends in 10 days." Identifying and
  accelerating this moment is critical for retention.

## Questions They Ask

1. "What percentage of our users would be 'very disappointed' if this
   product went away?"
2. "What does our retention curve look like? Does it flatten?"
3. "Who are our best users — the ones who retain longest — and what do
   they have in common?"
4. "What's the magic moment for new users? How quickly do they get there?"
5. "What's our growth loop? Is it sustainable, or are we buying growth?"
6. "What does 'good' look like for this metric? Do we have a benchmark?"
7. "Who's the ICP? Are we trying to serve too broad an audience?"
8. "What's the North Star metric, and how does this opportunity move it?"
9. "What are the leading indicators that tell us this is working before
   we see the lagging outcomes?"
10. "How does [company X] solve this? What can we learn from them?"
11. "What does the data say about why users churn?"
12. "Are we measuring input metrics (things we control) or just output
    metrics (things we hope for)?"
13. "What's the simplest version of this we could ship and measure?"
14. "Who have we talked to about this, and what pattern are we seeing?"

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Vanity metrics.** Measuring things that look good in a slide deck but
don't predict outcomes. Page views, registered users, app downloads. These
feel good and mean nothing without engagement and retention data behind them.

**Building without measuring.** Shipping features and never looking at
whether they moved a metric. Rachitsky wants every bet to have a
hypothesis and a measurement plan.

**Ignoring retention.** Obsessing over acquisition (new users!) while
ignoring retention (do they come back?). A leaky bucket can't be filled
by pouring faster. Retention is the foundational metric.

**Broad ICPs.** "Our product is for everyone" is a strategy for no one.
Rachitsky pushes teams to narrow their ICP, serve that segment
exceptionally well, and expand from a position of strength.

**Reinventing the wheel.** Not looking at how other companies solved
similar problems. Rachitsky's whole approach is built on learning from
examples. If Slack, Figma, and Notion all approached onboarding a
certain way, there's probably a reason.

**Gut-driven prioritization.** Deciding what to build based on intuition
or stakeholder loudness rather than data about customer value and
business impact. Rachitsky wants frameworks for prioritization, not
politics.

**Growth hacks without loops.** One-time acquisition tactics that don't
compound. Rachitsky pushes for structural growth loops — mechanisms
that create sustainable, compounding growth.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team knows their PMF score and tracks it over time.
- Retention is measured and benchmarked against relevant comparables.
- There's a clear ICP, and the team can describe who it is and why.
- Prioritization uses a framework (RICE, ICE, or similar) with real
  data, not just intuition.
- The team studies how other companies have solved similar problems
  before designing their solution.
- Metrics are specific, leading, and actionable — not vague and
  trailing.
- Growth is understood structurally (loops), not tactically (hacks).
- The team ships, measures, learns, and iterates. The cycle is fast
  and consistent.

## Implications for Etak

**When a user thinks like Rachitsky, Etak should:**

- Connect opportunities to metrics. When a user identifies an
  opportunity, Etak should ask: "How would we measure whether this
  is working? What metric moves?" Rachitsky-style thinkers want
  quantitative rigor in their discovery.
- Support benchmarking. When evaluating an opportunity, Etak might
  ask: "What does good look like here? Do we have a benchmark?"
  Help the user calibrate their expectations against industry
  standards.
- Reference patterns from other companies. Rachitsky's approach
  is deeply comparative. Etak could support this by helping users
  think about analogous products and what they did.
- Track experiments and their results. Not just "we tried this"
  but "here's what happened, here's what the data showed, here's
  what we concluded." Etak should support a lightweight experiment
  log alongside the opportunity tree.
- Integrate growth thinking with discovery. Rachitsky doesn't
  separate "product" from "growth." Etak should treat retention,
  activation, and growth loops as valid discovery concerns, not
  just post-launch optimization.
- Help narrow the ICP. When the user defines a broad opportunity,
  Etak might ask: "Who specifically? Which segment shows the
  strongest signal?"

**Risks to manage:**

- Data-driven thinking can crowd out qualitative insight. Not
  everything that matters is measurable, and Rachitsky-style
  users sometimes undervalue qualitative discovery. Etak should
  balance quantitative rigor with qualitative depth.
- Benchmark obsession can be misleading. Every product is unique,
  and what's "good" for Slack may not be relevant for a B2B
  infrastructure tool. Etak should present benchmarks as reference
  points, not targets.
- The curation/synthesis approach can lead to framework overload.
  A user who follows Rachitsky might know twelve prioritization
  frameworks and struggle to pick one. Etak should help simplify,
  not add to the pile.
