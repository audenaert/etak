# Shreyas Doshi

## Who They Are

Shreyas Doshi is a product leader who held senior PM roles at Stripe, Twitter,
Google, and Yahoo. He has not written a book; his influence comes from prolific,
opinionated writing on Twitter/X and through talks and courses. He is known for
sharp, contrarian thinking about what PMs actually spend their time on versus
what they should spend their time on. His key frameworks include LNO (Leverage,
Neutral, Overhead), pre-mortems, and his concept of "the maze." He teaches a
course called "The Art of Product Management." He represents a practitioner
tradition that prizes taste, judgment, speed, and clarity of thought over
process and methodology.

## Core Philosophy

Doshi's deepest conviction is that **most product managers are trapped in
low-leverage work and don't realize it.** They are busy — extraordinarily busy
— but their busyness is largely spent on Neutral and Overhead tasks (status
updates, stakeholder alignment, backlog grooming, roadmap presentations) while
the high-Leverage work (making the hard product calls, developing taste,
thinking deeply about what to build) gets squeezed into whatever time is left.

He calls this "the maze." Most PMs are solving problems that shouldn't exist —
navigating organizational complexity, building consensus for decisions that
should be obvious, managing stakeholders who shouldn't need managing. They are
lost in the maze of process and politics, and they mistake navigating the maze
for doing the actual work. The best PMs either avoid the maze entirely or
escape it quickly to spend their time on what actually matters: making the
product better.

Doshi believes in **opinionated products** — products that take a clear point
of view about how something should work, rather than trying to serve every
possible use case. This requires PMs with taste and the courage to say no. He
is deeply suspicious of design-by-committee, excessive user research that
substitutes for judgment, and consensus-driven product decisions.

He is not anti-process, but he is anti-process-as-substitute-for-thinking. A
framework is useful when it sharpens judgment; it is harmful when it replaces
judgment.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **LNO framework** — categorize all your work as Leverage (high-impact,
  do these exceptionally well), Neutral (necessary, do these adequately),
  or Overhead (bureaucratic, minimize these ruthlessly).
- **The maze** — the organizational complexity that traps PMs in low-
  leverage work. "Most PMs are solving problems that shouldn't exist."
- **Pre-mortem** — before committing to a plan, imagine it has failed and
  ask why. Surfaces risks that optimism bias hides.
- **Opinionated product** — a product with a clear point of view.
  "The best products are opinionated. They make choices so users don't
  have to."
- **Taste** — the cultivated ability to recognize what is good, what is
  right, what will resonate. Not teachable through frameworks.
- **High-agency PM** — a PM who makes things happen rather than waiting
  for permission, process, or consensus. Takes ownership.
- **PM theater** — the performative aspects of product management that
  look like work but produce no value. Status decks, roadmap
  presentations, prioritization frameworks used to justify decisions
  already made.
- **Conviction** — the willingness to make a bet on a direction even
  when the data is ambiguous. "Strong opinions, weakly held" but with
  emphasis on the "strong opinions" part.
- **Speed as a feature** — moving fast is not just efficiency; it is a
  strategic advantage. Slow discovery is often worse than imperfect
  discovery.
- **"What is the actual problem?"** — Doshi frequently cuts through
  complexity by insisting on clarity about the real problem before
  discussing solutions or process.
- **Defaults** — Doshi thinks a lot about choosing the right defaults,
  both in products and in how teams operate.

## Questions They Ask

1. Is this Leverage, Neutral, or Overhead work — and are you spending
   your best energy on the Leverage work?
2. What is the actual problem here? Not the symptom, not the stakeholder
   request — the actual problem.
3. If this initiative fails, why will it have failed? (Pre-mortem.)
4. Are you in the maze right now? Is the problem you're solving a real
   product problem or an organizational one?
5. What would an opinionated version of this product look like? What would
   you cut if you had the courage?
6. Are you using this framework because it helps you think, or because it
   makes you feel rigorous?
7. If you could only ship one thing this quarter, what would it be and why?
8. What decision are you avoiding making?
9. Is this meeting, document, or process actually necessary, or is it PM
   theater?
10. Do you have conviction about this direction, or are you hedging because
    the data is ambiguous?
11. What would a high-agency PM do in this situation?
12. Who is this product for — specifically? Not "everyone" — who is the
    person whose life gets meaningfully better?
13. Are you moving slowly because the problem is hard, or because the
    organization is slow?
14. What is the default here, and is it the right one?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

- **PM theater.** The entire apparatus of performative product management:
  elaborate prioritization frameworks, roadmap review meetings, stack-
  ranked backlogs — all theater when the real decisions are made elsewhere
  or when they substitute for actual product thinking.
- **Death by consensus.** Products designed by committee that try to please
  everyone and delight no one. The absence of conviction in product
  decisions.
- **Over-process.** Using frameworks, rituals, and ceremonies as a
  substitute for judgment. Doshi is especially critical of PMs who hide
  behind "the process says..." instead of making a call.
- **The maze.** PMs spending most of their time navigating organizational
  complexity — stakeholder management, cross-functional alignment,
  political negotiation — instead of thinking about the product.
- **Mistaking busyness for impact.** The PM who works 60 hours a week but
  spends most of it on Neutral and Overhead tasks while Leverage work
  remains undone.
- **Research as procrastination.** Using "we need more data" as an excuse
  to avoid making a decision. At some point you have to develop a point
  of view and commit.
- **Lack of taste.** Products that are functionally correct but feel
  wrong — clunky, incoherent, undifferentiated. The PM who can execute
  a process but can't recognize quality.
- **Feature parity thinking.** Building something because a competitor has
  it, rather than because it serves your users and your product's point
  of view.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The PM can clearly articulate the highest-leverage work on their plate
  and is spending disproportionate energy on it.
- Product decisions reflect a clear point of view. The product says no
  to things. It has taste.
- Pre-mortems are a natural part of planning. The team actively imagines
  failure and addresses the most likely causes.
- The PM moves fast. Discovery doesn't take months. Decisions don't wait
  for perfect data.
- Organizational complexity is managed but not mistaken for product work.
  The PM knows when they're in the maze and gets out quickly.
- The PM has genuine conviction about the direction, even when the data
  is ambiguous, and can explain the reasoning behind that conviction.
- Process exists to support thinking, not to replace it. Lightweight,
  purposeful, and discarded when it stops being useful.
- The product feels coherent — like it was designed by someone with a
  point of view, not assembled by a committee.

## Implications for Etak

**Emphasize:**
- Speed and clarity. Etak should help users cut through complexity and get
  to the core question fast. Long setup, heavy onboarding, or required
  fields before you can start thinking will drive this user away.
- Judgment support, not judgment replacement. Etak should help users think
  more clearly, not tell them what to think. It should sharpen conviction,
  not substitute for it.
- The LNO lens. Help users recognize when they're spending time on
  Overhead work within Etak itself. The tool should be relentlessly
  focused on Leverage activities.
- Pre-mortem thinking. When a user is converging on a solution, prompt
  them to imagine failure. This is natural to Doshi-influenced thinking.

**De-emphasize:**
- Heavy methodology. If Etak feels like a system with required steps,
  mandatory artifacts, and a prescribed workflow, this user will rebel.
  Etak should feel like a sharp tool, not a process.
- Documentation for its own sake. Doshi-influenced users want to think
  and decide, not produce artifacts. Any artifact Etak produces should
  serve thinking, not compliance.
- Consensus-building features. This user wants to make a call, not build
  alignment through process.

**Watch out for:**
- A Doshi-influenced user will be the first to call out if Etak itself
  is becoming PM theater — if using the tool feels productive but isn't
  actually helping them make better product decisions.
- They will resist any feature that adds process without adding clarity.
  Every interaction should earn its keep.
- They may push back on the OST structure if it feels like mandatory
  formalism rather than a thinking aid. Etak's incremental formalism
  approach — where structure emerges from conversation rather than being
  imposed upfront — is critical for this user.
- They will value Etak having an opinion. A tool that always asks "what
  do you think?" without ever offering a perspective will feel weak.

**Delight:**
- A tool that helps them identify what NOT to do — what to cut, what to
  stop, what to ignore — would match their mental model perfectly.
- Speed of interaction. If Etak can match the speed of their thinking —
  quick exchanges, rapid reframing, no friction — it will earn their
  trust.
- Helping them escape the maze. If Etak can recognize when a user is
  describing an organizational problem rather than a product problem and
  help them refocus, that would be deeply valuable.
- A tool that develops its own "taste" — that can recognize when a
  product direction feels incoherent or when a solution is trying to
  please everyone — would resonate with their values.
