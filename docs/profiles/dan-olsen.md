# Dan Olsen

> Author of *The Lean Product Playbook*. Methodical, framework-driven product thinker
> who believes product-market fit is achievable through a repeatable process.

---

## Who They Are

Dan Olsen is a product management consultant, author, and speaker best known for
*The Lean Product Playbook* (2015). He has a background in engineering (Caltech,
Stanford MBA) and has worked as a PM at Intuit and as head of product at several
startups. He runs a consultancy that helps teams find product-market fit.

His central contribution is making product-market fit feel achievable rather than
accidental. Where many PMs treat PMF as a mysterious thing that either happens or
doesn't, Olsen offers a structured, layer-by-layer approach. He draws heavily on
lean startup thinking but adds rigor and specificity that Ries left implicit.

He's the person teams call when they know something is off with their product but
can't articulate what layer the problem lives in.

---

## Core Philosophy

Product-market fit is not binary, not accidental, and not a single moment. It is a
continuous pursuit with identifiable layers, and you can work on each layer
deliberately.

The most common mistake product teams make is conflating the problem space with the
solution space. Olsen insists on separating these cleanly: understand the customer
and their underserved needs *before* you ever discuss features. Most teams jump to
solutions too fast because it feels productive. It isn't.

He believes in a **repeatable process**. Not rigid or waterfall-ish, but structured
enough that you can diagnose where things went wrong. If your product isn't working,
it's because one of the layers in the pyramid is off. Figure out which layer, fix
it, iterate.

Olsen trusts frameworks. He believes that good frameworks reduce cognitive load and
help teams have better conversations. A framework doesn't replace judgment, but it
channels it.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Product-Market Fit Pyramid** — the five-layer model: target customer,
  underserved needs, value proposition, feature set, UX. Each layer depends on the
  one below it.
- **Problem space vs. solution space** — the fundamental distinction. Problem space
  is about customer needs. Solution space is about your product. You must live in
  both, but never confuse which one you're in.
- **Importance vs. satisfaction framework** — plot customer needs on two axes. High
  importance + low satisfaction = opportunity. This is where you play.
- **Must-have score** — survey metric: "How would you feel if you could no longer
  use this product?" The percentage who say "very disappointed" is your must-have
  score. 40%+ suggests PMF.
- **Kano model** — needs are not all the same type. Performance needs (more is
  better), must-haves (expected, no delight), and delighters (unexpected, high
  delight). Know which type you're working on.
- **Underserved needs** — needs where importance exceeds satisfaction. The gap is
  the opportunity.
- **Value proposition** — the specific set of needs you choose to address and how
  well you'll address them relative to alternatives.
- **Feature set** — the solution-space translation of your value proposition.
  Features should map cleanly to needs.
- **Iterate in the problem space first** — don't build until you've validated
  the need.

## Questions They Ask

1. "Which layer of the pyramid are we working on right now?"
2. "Have we validated the target customer, or are we assuming?"
3. "What are the underserved needs here — high importance, low satisfaction?"
4. "Are we in problem space or solution space right now?"
5. "How does this feature map back to a specific customer need?"
6. "What's our must-have score? Do we have one?"
7. "Who are the alternatives, and where do they fall on the importance-satisfaction
   grid?"
8. "Is this a must-have, a performance need, or a delighter?"
9. "What would we need to believe for this to be worth building?"
10. "Have we talked to enough customers in our target segment to feel confident in
    this need?"
11. "What's the value proposition — stated simply, not as a feature list?"
12. "Where in the pyramid did our last product bet fail, and why?"
13. "Are we iterating on the right layer, or are we polishing UX when the need
    isn't validated?"

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Jumping to solutions.** The single biggest failure mode. A stakeholder says
"we need a dashboard" and the team starts designing a dashboard. No one asked
what problem the dashboard solves or whether a dashboard is the right shape for
the solution. Olsen wants you to stay in problem space longer than feels
comfortable.

**Treating all needs as equal.** Not distinguishing between must-haves,
performance needs, and delighters leads to bloated products that do many things
adequately and nothing exceptionally.

**Vague target customers.** "Small businesses" is not a target customer. Olsen
pushes for specificity — who exactly, in what context, with what existing
behavior?

**Skipping layers.** Teams that jump from "we know our customer" straight to
"here's the feature set" without articulating the underserved needs and value
proposition. Each layer is load-bearing.

**Confusing correlation with validation.** A customer says "I'd use that" and
the team marks the need as validated. Olsen wants behavioral evidence, not just
stated preference.

**Feature soup roadmaps.** Roadmaps that are lists of features with no
connecting logic back to needs and value proposition. You can't tell why these
features and not others.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team can articulate each layer of the pyramid for their product, from
  target customer through UX, and explain how the layers connect.
- Discovery conversations explicitly distinguish problem space from solution
  space. Someone on the team flags it when the group drifts.
- Customer needs are mapped on importance vs. satisfaction. The team can point
  to the quadrant they're targeting and explain why.
- Features trace back to specific needs. If you ask "why this feature?" the
  answer is a customer need, not a stakeholder request.
- The team iterates at the right layer. If the problem is unclear, they don't
  waste time on UX polish. If the problem is validated, they focus on solution
  quality.
- There's a must-have score, and the team tracks it over time.

## Implications for Etak

**When a user thinks like Olsen, Etak should:**

- Make the problem space / solution space distinction explicit. When a user
  starts describing features, Etak might ask: "Are we in solution space now?
  Should we be?"
- Support layered reasoning. The opportunity solution tree maps naturally to
  Olsen's pyramid — outcomes map to needs, solutions map to the feature set
  layer. Etak should help users see which layer they're operating in.
- Offer the importance-satisfaction framing when prioritizing opportunities.
  "How important is this need to the customer? How well is it served today?"
- Resist premature convergence on solutions. For Olsen-style thinkers, Etak
  should be comfortable staying in problem space for multiple turns of
  conversation.
- Track the value proposition as a first-class artifact. Not just a list of
  features, but a statement about which needs are being addressed and to what
  degree.
- Support Kano-style classification of needs. When the user adds opportunities
  to the tree, Etak could ask: "Is this a must-have, a performance need, or a
  delighter?"

**Risks to manage:**

- Olsen-style thinking can become overly mechanical. Etak should use the
  frameworks as conversation tools, not checklists. The goal is better thinking,
  not completed templates.
- The pyramid is a model, not reality. Etak should hold it loosely — some
  products don't decompose neatly into five layers, and that's fine.
