# Des Traynor

> Design reference profile for Etak product discovery adaptation.

---

## Who They Are

Des Traynor is co-founder and Chief Strategy Officer of Intercom, a customer
messaging platform he helped build from a Dublin startup into one of the
defining product-led growth companies of the 2010s. He is a prolific writer
and speaker on product strategy, customer research, and Jobs to Be Done
(JTBD). Before Intercom, he was a UX consultant and lecturer.

Traynor is the practitioner's voice for JTBD thinking. While Clayton
Christensen originated the theory and Bob Moesta refined the interview
methodology, Traynor translated JTBD into everyday product decision-making
through years of writing on the Intercom blog, conference talks, and the book
*Intercom on Jobs to Be Done*. He is direct, opinionated, and allergic to
abstraction that doesn't lead to action. His writing is clear, often
provocative, and grounded in what he learned building Intercom.

Key contributions:
- Practical application of JTBD to product roadmapping and prioritization
- The "forces of progress" diagram (push, pull, anxiety, habit)
- Writing on feature requests, customer segmentation, and product-led growth
- The "cupcake vs. wedding cake" model of minimum viable products
- Extensive thinking on when to say "no" to feature requests

## Core Philosophy

Customers don't buy products. They hire them to make progress in their lives.
The unit of analysis for product decisions is not the feature, the persona, or
the market segment — it is the *job* the customer is trying to get done. If
you understand the job, you can build the right product. If you don't, you'll
build the wrong one regardless of how well you execute.

The struggling moment is everything. A customer switches products (or adopts
a new one) because they experience a moment where their current situation is
no longer acceptable. Something pushes them away from the status quo, and
something pulls them toward a new solution. But switching also encounters
resistance: the anxiety of the new ("will this actually work?") and the
comfort of the old ("I know how to use what I have now"). These four forces
— push, pull, anxiety, habit — determine whether a customer makes a change.

Feature requests are not jobs. When a customer says "I want a calendar view,"
they are describing a solution, not a job. The job might be "I need to see
when my team is available so I can schedule a meeting without a ten-email
thread." The solution might be a calendar view, or it might be something
completely different. You must get past the request to the underlying
struggle.

Products should do one job really well before trying to do many jobs. The
temptation to expand into adjacent jobs is strong, but premature expansion
dilutes focus and creates a product that is mediocre at everything. Nail the
core job first. Expand deliberately.

Say no to almost everything. Most feature requests, most market
opportunities, most partnership ideas — the answer should be no. Not because
they're bad ideas, but because focus requires exclusion. Every "yes" is a
"no" to something else, and most teams say "yes" too often.

## Discovery Vocabulary

**Signature terms and phrases:**
- "Job to be done" (JTBD) — the progress a customer is trying to make
- "Struggling moment" — the trigger point where the current way stops working
- "Forces of progress" — push, pull, anxiety, habit — the four forces that
  determine switching behavior
- "Push" — what's driving the customer away from their current solution
- "Pull" — what's attracting them toward the new solution
- "Anxiety" — fear that the new solution won't work or will be too hard
- "Habit" — comfort with the existing way of doing things
- "Hiring and firing" — customers hire products for a job and fire them when
  something better comes along
- "Switching costs" — everything the customer gives up to adopt something new
- "Feature requests are not jobs" — the distinction between solutions and needs
- "Outcome-driven" — focusing on the customer's desired outcome, not features

**Characteristic phrasing:**
- "People don't want a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole."
- "What job is the customer hiring your product to do?"
- "When was the last time you actually watched someone struggle with this?"
- "That's a feature request, not a job. What's the underlying struggle?"
- "What would your customer do if your product didn't exist? That's your
  real competition."
- "Every feature you add is a decision you're making for every future customer."
- "If the answer is 'everyone needs this,' you haven't done the work yet."
- "You're trying to build a wedding cake when you should be perfecting a cupcake."

**Metaphors:**
- Hiring and firing products (like hiring an employee for a specific role)
- The four forces as a tug-of-war between change and status quo
- Cupcake vs. wedding cake (nail something small before going big)
- Products as solutions looking for matching problems

## Questions They Ask

1. What job is the customer hiring this product to do? State it as a verb — what progress are they trying to make?
2. What is the struggling moment? When exactly does the customer feel that their current approach is no longer good enough?
3. What's pushing them away from the status quo? What pain or frustration is making the old way unacceptable?
4. What's pulling them toward a new solution? What does the better future look like in their mind?
5. What anxiety do they have about switching? What are they afraid will go wrong? What will they lose?
6. What habits are keeping them in the current solution? What's comfortable about the status quo that a new solution must overcome?
7. When a customer requests this feature, what's the job underneath the request? What struggle are they actually trying to resolve?
8. What would the customer do if this product didn't exist? That alternative — however crude — is your real competitor.
9. Are you nailing the core job before expanding to adjacent ones? Or are you spreading into new jobs before the first one is truly solved?
10. How many jobs is this product trying to do? Is it trying to be a cupcake or a wedding cake?
11. Who are you saying no to? What requests are you explicitly declining, and why?
12. Have you actually watched someone experience the struggling moment? Not surveyed them — watched them?
13. What is the switching cost for the customer? Is it higher than the pain of staying where they are?
14. Is this a job people do frequently or rarely? Because the adoption strategy is completely different.

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Building features instead of solving jobs.** Adding capabilities because
customers asked for them or because competitors have them, without
understanding the underlying job. Feature parity thinking. "They have it,
so we need it" is not a product strategy.

**Confusing feature requests with customer needs.** Taking customer requests
at face value. "They asked for a calendar" becomes a calendar on the roadmap,
without ever asking *why* they want a calendar. The request is a symptom;
the job is the cause.

**Trying to serve everyone.** "Our target market is anyone who..." This
means you don't have a target market. Products that try to serve every
customer serve none of them well. The best products are opinionated about
who they're for.

**Premature expansion.** Expanding into adjacent jobs before the core job is
nailed. Building the wedding cake before the cupcake is delicious. This
usually happens because of investor pressure, competitive anxiety, or
boredom with the core job.

**Ignoring switching costs.** Building a product that is better than the
alternative but not enough better to overcome the cost of switching. The
new solution must not just be better — it must be so much better that
customers will endure the pain of migration, relearning, and uncertainty.

**Solution-first discovery.** Starting with "we should build X" instead of
"customers struggle with Y." The solution comes before the problem. This
leads to products that are technically impressive but don't connect with
any real struggle.

**Survey-driven research.** Asking customers what they want via surveys
instead of observing them in context. Surveys capture opinions; observation
captures behavior. Behavior is what matters.

**The "just one more feature" fallacy.** Believing that the next feature will
unlock growth. If the core job isn't being done well, adding features makes
the product more complex without making it more valuable.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- The team can articulate the core job their product is hired to do in one
  clear sentence, using a verb. It describes progress the customer wants to
  make, not a feature the product has.
- Customer research includes observation of struggling moments — not just
  interviews, but watching people encounter the problem in context.
- Feature requests are treated as signals, not specifications. When a
  customer asks for something, the team's first response is to understand
  the underlying job, not to add it to the backlog.
- The forces of progress (push, pull, anxiety, habit) are mapped for key
  customer segments. The team understands not just what's attracting
  customers but what's preventing them from switching.
- The team says "no" frequently and can explain why. They have a clear
  sense of what the product does and does not do, and they protect that
  focus.
- The product does one job exceptionally well before expanding to adjacent
  jobs. Expansion is deliberate and connected to the core, not reactive.
- Switching costs are understood and actively managed. Onboarding, migration
  tools, and progressive disclosure are designed to reduce anxiety and habit
  forces.
- The roadmap is organized around jobs, not features. Priorities are set
  based on which jobs are most underserved, not which features are most
  requested.

## Implications for Etak

**Job-first opportunity framing.** When a Traynor-aligned user describes an
opportunity, Etak should frame it as a job. "What progress is the customer
trying to make?" The opportunity tree should be organized around jobs, not
features or market segments.

**Struggling moment as entry point.** Start discovery by identifying the
struggling moment. "When does the customer feel that their current approach
isn't working? What triggers that feeling?" This is the root of the
opportunity — everything else flows from understanding the struggle.

**Forces of progress mapping.** Etak should help the user map the four forces
for each opportunity. "What's pushing them away from the status quo? What's
pulling them toward a solution? What anxiety do they have? What habits are
keeping them in place?" This is a natural structuring tool for the
opportunity solution tree.

**Feature-to-job translation.** When the user describes a solution as a
feature, Etak should prompt them to translate it into job language. "You
said 'add a calendar view.' What's the job the customer would hire the
calendar view to do?" This reflex prevents solution-first thinking.

**Focus enforcement.** Traynor-aligned users believe in doing one thing well.
When the opportunity tree expands into multiple jobs, Etak should flag it.
"You're exploring three different jobs now. Which one is the core job
you want to nail first?"

**Switching cost awareness.** When evaluating solutions, Etak should ask
about switching costs. "How hard is it for the customer to adopt this?
What are they giving up? Is the improvement big enough to overcome the
inertia?" Solutions that don't clear the switching cost bar won't be
adopted, regardless of quality.

**Incremental formalism alignment.** Strong alignment. JTBD thinking is
naturally conversational — the best job insights come from open-ended
customer interviews, not structured surveys. Etak should start with loose
dialogue about the customer's world and gradually crystallize into job
statements, force maps, and prioritized opportunities. The formalism
should emerge from the conversation, not precede it.

**Observation over opinion.** Etak should encourage the user to ground
claims in observation. "Have you watched someone experience this? What
did you see?" This pushes toward behavioral evidence rather than
self-reported preferences.
