# Hope Gurion

> Founder of Fearless Product. Product leadership coach.
> Focuses on the human and organizational conditions that make discovery possible.

---

## Who They Are

Hope Gurion is the founder of Fearless Product, a coaching practice for product
leaders. She has over 20 years of product leadership experience, including VP of
Product roles at CarGurus, Jibo, and Pivotal Software. She is a frequent speaker,
writes about product leadership, and coaches CPOs, VPs of Product, and senior PMs.

Her contribution is distinct from most product thinkers: she focuses on the
**leadership layer** — how product leaders develop their teams, create the
conditions for good discovery, and navigate the organizational dynamics that
determine whether discovery actually happens. While Torres teaches the mechanics
of discovery and Cagan describes the empowered team model, Gurion focuses on
how leaders make those things real inside imperfect organizations.

She's the person you call when you're a newly promoted VP of Product and you
realize that knowing how to do product work is different from knowing how to lead
a product organization.

## Core Philosophy

Good product outcomes depend more on organizational conditions than on individual
PM skill. A talented PM in a broken organization will produce mediocre work. A
developing PM in a well-led organization will grow quickly and produce good work.

Product leadership is fundamentally about **creating the conditions** for good
discovery. That means: setting clear outcomes, giving teams autonomy to pursue
those outcomes, building psychological safety so people can be wrong without fear,
coaching PMs to develop judgment rather than just following process, and shielding
teams from organizational dysfunction.

She believes deeply in **product trios** — the PM, designer, and tech lead working
together as a collaborative unit. Not the PM as the "mini-CEO" who dictates, but
a genuine partnership where each discipline brings irreplaceable perspective. The
leader's job is to foster this collaboration, not to replace it.

Gurion emphasizes **outcome-oriented roadmaps** over feature-based ones. A roadmap
should communicate the problems you intend to solve and the outcomes you expect,
not the features you plan to build. This gives teams room to discover the right
solutions.

She is empathetic and practical. She understands that most product organizations
are not ideal, and she meets leaders where they are. She doesn't demand that
everyone become Spotify overnight. She helps leaders take the next step from
wherever they stand.

## Discovery Vocabulary

- **Product trio** — PM, designer, and tech lead working as a collaborative
  unit. Not three silos that hand off to each other. They discover together,
  decide together, and share accountability.
- **Outcome-oriented roadmap** — a roadmap organized around problems to solve
  and outcomes to achieve, not features to build. Communicates intent without
  prescribing solutions.
- **Product judgment** — the developed instinct for what will work, built
  through experience, reflection, and coaching. Not a replacement for research,
  but a complement to it. Leaders develop this in their teams.
- **Psychological safety** — the belief that you can take risks, be wrong, and
  speak up without punishment. Essential for discovery, which requires admitting
  uncertainty and changing your mind.
- **Coaching stance** — the leader's default posture. Ask questions before
  giving answers. Help PMs develop their own reasoning rather than giving them
  your conclusions.
- **Empowered teams** — teams given outcomes to achieve and the autonomy to
  figure out how. The opposite of feature teams that execute a predetermined
  roadmap.
- **Fearless product** — her framing for product work done without the anxiety
  of needing to be right the first time. Discovery is inherently uncertain;
  leaders must make it safe to be uncertain.
- **Product leadership operating system** — the rhythms, rituals, and
  structures that a product leader puts in place: how strategy is communicated,
  how priorities are set, how teams report progress, how feedback is given.
- **Stakeholder partnership** — not stakeholder management (which implies
  manipulation), but genuine partnership. Helping stakeholders understand
  discovery and including them in learning.

## Questions They Ask

1. "Does the team understand the outcome they're pursuing, or just the
   features they've been asked to build?"
2. "How is the product trio collaborating? Are all three disciplines
   shaping the discovery work?"
3. "What's on your roadmap — features or problems to solve?"
4. "When was the last time a team changed direction based on what they
   learned in discovery?"
5. "How do you give feedback to your PMs? What does that look like?"
6. "What happens when a team tries something and it doesn't work? How
   does the organization respond?"
7. "Does the team feel safe being wrong?"
8. "Are you coaching your PMs or just reviewing their output?"
9. "What does the team's weekly rhythm look like? Where does discovery
   fit in?"
10. "How are you communicating strategy so that teams can make good
    autonomous decisions?"
11. "What's the biggest organizational impediment to good discovery
    right now?"
12. "Are your stakeholders partners in discovery, or are they waiting
    for the team to deliver their requests?"
13. "How are you developing product judgment in your team, not just
    process compliance?"
14. "What would your PMs say if I asked them whether they feel
    empowered?"

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**The PM as order-taker.** When PMs receive feature requests from stakeholders
and treat them as requirements. Gurion pushes leaders to reframe the
relationship: stakeholders bring problems and context, PMs bring solutions
and evidence.

**Feature-factory roadmaps.** Roadmaps that are lists of features with dates.
No outcomes, no connection to strategy, no room for discovery. Gurion works
with leaders to shift toward outcome-oriented planning.

**Absentee leadership.** Product leaders who set goals and disappear, checking
in only at review time. Gurion emphasizes consistent coaching, regular 1:1s,
and being present in the work without micromanaging.

**Hero culture.** Organizations that celebrate the PM who had the brilliant
idea, rather than the trio that ran great discovery. This incentivizes
individual heroics over collaborative learning.

**Discovery theater.** Teams that go through the motions of discovery — they
do interviews, they have a tree — but the decisions are already made. The
discovery is performative, not genuine. Gurion calls this out gently but
firmly.

**Feedback avoidance.** Leaders who don't give direct feedback because they
don't want to hurt feelings. Gurion believes clear, kind, specific feedback
is one of the most important things a product leader does.

**Copying someone else's process.** Adopting Spotify's model or Google's
sprint methodology without adapting it to your context. Gurion helps leaders
build their own operating system based on their team's needs and maturity.

## What "Good" Looks Like

- Product trios work as genuine collaborators, not three people doing
  their own work in sequence.
- The roadmap describes problems and outcomes. Teams have latitude in
  how they solve them.
- PMs can articulate why they're pursuing a particular opportunity,
  and the reasoning includes customer evidence.
- The leader's 1:1s are coaching conversations, not status updates.
- When a bet doesn't work out, the team reflects and adjusts without
  blame.
- Stakeholders understand the discovery process and participate
  constructively — they bring context and constraints, not solutions.
- PMs are growing. You can see their judgment improving over quarters.
- The team's weekly rhythm includes dedicated discovery time, not just
  delivery work.

## Implications for Etak

**When a user thinks like Gurion, Etak should:**

- Support the coaching conversation. Gurion-style users may be product
  leaders helping their PMs think through discovery, not doing the
  discovery themselves. Etak should be useful in a coaching context —
  asking questions, surfacing thinking gaps, helping the PM develop
  their own reasoning.
- Emphasize outcomes over features in the opportunity solution tree.
  The top of the tree should be outcomes, and Etak should gently
  resist when users try to start with solutions.
- Make the trio visible. When discussing discovery, Etak might ask:
  "Has the designer weighed in on this? What's the tech lead's
  perspective?" Discovery is a team activity.
- Support organizational context. Gurion-style users think about
  discovery within an organizational system — stakeholder dynamics,
  team maturity, leadership structures. Etak should accommodate
  this context rather than assuming a vacuum.
- Track learning, not just decisions. Gurion cares about whether the
  team is developing judgment over time. Etak could help surface
  patterns: "The last three discoveries in this area led to pivots —
  what's the pattern?"
- Be a safe space for uncertainty. Gurion's "fearless" framing means
  Etak should normalize not knowing. "We're not sure yet" is a valid
  state, not a failure.

**Risks to manage:**

- Gurion's focus on leadership and organization can pull away from the
  tactical work of discovery. Etak should balance the meta-conversation
  (how is the team doing?) with the object-level work (what are we
  learning about customers?).
- Not every Etak user is a product leader. Gurion's perspective is most
  relevant when the user is coaching or leading, less so when they're
  an individual contributor doing hands-on discovery.
