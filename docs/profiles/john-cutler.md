# John Cutler

> Design reference profile for Etak product discovery adaptation.

---

## Who They Are

John Cutler is a product thinker and writer currently at Toast, previously at
Amplitude, Pendo, and several other product-led companies. He is best known for
his prolific output on product management, organizational dynamics, and the
systems that shape how teams actually work. His platform of choice has shifted
over the years — Medium, Twitter/X, Substack ("The Beautiful Mess") — but the
throughline is always the same: how do organizational structures create (or
prevent) good product outcomes?

He is not primarily a framework author. He is a systems thinker who draws
complex diagrams showing feedback loops, cognitive load, work-in-progress
dynamics, and the hidden forces that turn well-intentioned teams into feature
factories. His writing is empathetic — he understands that most PMs are not
failing because they lack skill, but because they are embedded in systems that
make good work structurally difficult.

Key works and contributions:
- "12 Signs You're Working in a Feature Factory" (widely shared essay)
- "The Beautiful Mess" newsletter
- Extensive writing on WIP limits, cognitive load, and org design
- Complex systems diagrams that map product dysfunction

## Core Philosophy

Product work is inseparable from the organizational system it happens within.
You cannot improve outcomes by giving individuals better frameworks if the
system they operate in punishes the behavior those frameworks require.

Discovery is not a phase or a ceremony — it is a continuous activity that
happens (or fails to happen) because of how the organization structures
incentives, information flow, and decision rights. The "feature factory" is not
caused by bad PMs; it is caused by systems that reward output over outcomes,
that fragment ownership, and that treat product teams as internal service desks.

Healthy product work requires:
- Low work-in-progress (fewer things in flight, more things finished)
- Explicit bets with learning loops (not just "shipped it")
- Teams that own outcomes, not just output
- Psychological safety to say "we don't know yet"
- Strategy that is a living thing, not a document nobody reads

The work *is* the strategy. What you choose to work on, how you sequence it,
and what you choose *not* to work on reveals your actual strategy — regardless
of what any deck says.

## Discovery Vocabulary

**Signature terms and phrases:**
- "Feature factory" — teams that ship features without connecting to outcomes
- "The work is the strategy" — strategy is revealed by what you actually do
- "WIP limits" — constraining work-in-progress to improve flow and focus
- "Healthy tension" — the productive friction between bets and learning
- "Cognitive load" — the hidden tax of too many priorities and context switches
- "Output vs. outcomes" — shipping things vs. achieving results
- "Productivity theater" — looking busy vs. making progress
- "The system" — the organizational structures that shape behavior
- "Bets" — work framed as hypotheses, not commitments
- "Learning loops" — structured ways to validate whether bets are paying off
- "North star metric" — a single metric that captures customer value creation
- "Enabling constraints" — limitations that actually improve decision-making

**Characteristic phrasing:**
- "What's the system producing this behavior?"
- "How many things do you have in flight right now?"
- "What did you learn from the last thing you shipped?"
- "Is this a bet or a commitment? Those require different approaches."
- "The org chart is the first product constraint."
- "What would it look like if the team actually owned this outcome?"

**Metaphors:**
- Product orgs as complex adaptive systems
- WIP as inventory — it has carrying costs
- Strategy as a river, not a document
- Feature factories as assembly lines disconnected from customers

## Questions They Ask

1. How many things does this team have in flight right now? What would happen if you cut that in half?
2. What is the organizational system that is producing this pattern? Is the team responding rationally to bad incentives?
3. When you shipped the last three features, what did you learn? Did anything change as a result of that learning?
4. Is this a bet or a commitment? If it's a bet, what would cause you to walk away from it?
5. Who actually owns this outcome? Or does everyone "contribute" and nobody owns it?
6. What is the cognitive load on this team right now? How many contexts are they switching between?
7. What would this look like if the team had real autonomy over the outcome, not just the output?
8. How does information flow from customers back to the people making product decisions? How many hops does it take?
9. What is the healthiest tension in this organization right now? What tension has become unhealthy?
10. If I asked the team what the strategy is, would they all say the same thing? Would it match what leadership says?
11. What are you explicitly choosing *not* to do? Is that decision legible to the team?
12. How are you distinguishing between "we tried this and it didn't work" and "we shipped this and never checked"?
13. What is the shortest feedback loop available to this team? Are they using it?
14. Is this discovery work, or is this justification for a decision that's already been made?

## Anti-Patterns They Fight

**Feature factory behavior.** Teams that measure success by number of features
shipped. No connection between output and outcomes. Roadmaps that are just
lists of features with dates. PMs who function as project managers for
stakeholder requests.

**Productivity theater.** Busy teams that look productive but aren't making
progress on things that matter. Lots of ceremonies, lots of tickets, lots of
"velocity" — but no evidence of customer value creation.

**WIP explosion.** Too many things in flight. Every initiative is "in progress,"
nothing is finished. Teams context-switching between five priorities and making
slow progress on all of them.

**Strategy-execution gap.** Beautiful strategy decks that have no connection to
what teams actually work on. Strategy as an annual exercise rather than a
living, breathing set of decisions.

**Learned helplessness.** Teams that have internalized that they can't influence
what they work on, so they stop trying. PMs who describe themselves as
"ticket takers" or "order takers" and see that as inevitable.

**Premature convergence.** Jumping to solutions before understanding the
problem space. "We already know what to build" as a way to avoid the
discomfort of uncertainty.

**Blaming individuals for systemic problems.** Firing the PM instead of fixing
the system. "We need better people" instead of "we need a better system."

## What "Good" Looks Like

- Teams can articulate what they are trying to learn, not just what they are
  trying to ship.
- Work-in-progress is low. The team is finishing things, not starting things.
- There is a visible connection between strategy, bets, and the work the team
  is doing right now.
- Teams have real ownership of outcomes, not just output. They can make
  decisions about how to achieve the outcome without asking permission.
- Learning loops are short. Teams ship small things, observe the results, and
  adjust. This is not a quarterly cycle — it is continuous.
- The team can tell you what they are explicitly *not* doing and why.
- Healthy tension exists between leadership's strategic bets and the team's
  learning from the ground. Neither side dominates.
- PMs spend meaningful time with customers — not just in scheduled research
  sessions, but as a regular part of how they work.
- The system rewards learning, not just shipping. A team that ran a good
  experiment and learned "don't build this" is celebrated, not punished.

## Implications for Etak

**System awareness.** When working with a Cutler-aligned thinker, Etak should
surface systemic context. "You've added a fourth opportunity to explore — is
this team's WIP getting too high?" Help the user see the system, not just the
individual decision.

**Bets framing.** Frame work as bets, not commitments. Etak should ask: "What
would you need to see to walk away from this?" and "What is this bet trying to
teach you?" The opportunity solution tree should feel like a portfolio of bets
with explicit learning goals.

**Output-outcome connection.** Continuously prompt the user to connect features
to outcomes. When they add a solution, ask what outcome it serves. When they
describe an outcome, ask how they'll know it's happening.

**WIP visibility.** Make work-in-progress visible. Show how many things are
being explored simultaneously. Gently flag when WIP is climbing without
things being resolved.

**Empathy for the PM's context.** Cutler-aligned users are often working within
difficult organizational systems. Etak should not be preachy about "best
practices." Instead, help them navigate the system they're in — acknowledge
constraints, help them frame arguments for better ways of working, and
support them in making the case for outcome-oriented work.

**Incremental formalism alignment.** This profile aligns strongly with Etak's
core philosophy. Cutler-aligned thinkers will appreciate starting with loose
dialogue and gradually crystallizing. Don't push for premature structure.
Let the system map emerge from the conversation.

**Strategy-as-practice.** Help the user see how their discovery work connects
to (or diverges from) stated strategy. The opportunity tree is a strategy
artifact — surface that connection explicitly.
