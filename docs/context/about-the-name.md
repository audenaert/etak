# About the name

**For:** anyone who wants to understand the philosophy behind the tool. **Length:** about four minutes. **Matters because:** the name captures the shift in frame that makes Etak different from other discovery tools. If you only read one piece of context, read this one.

---

**Etak** (*ay-tahk*) rhymes with "day rock." Two syllables, even stress.

Etak is a concept from Polynesian navigation. It describes a method for tracking position at sea without instruments, charts, or a fixed map, developed over centuries in Micronesia and across the Pacific. The method is cognitively strange, mechanically precise, and directly relevant to the problem this product exists to solve.

## The technique

Western navigation treats the traveler as a moving point on a fixed map. You mark your position. You advance along a route. The world stays still; you move through it.

Etak inverts this. The navigator holds themselves fixed. The islands move.

The method works by triangulation against a reference island: not the origin, not the destination, but a third island off to the side. The reference island is almost never visible. It may be hundreds of miles away. But the navigator knows, from oral tradition passed down through generations of apprenticeship, exactly where it lies relative to the stars. As the canoe sails, the navigator tracks the star-bearing of this invisible reference island. As that bearing shifts, as the reference island appears to slide under different stars, the navigator knows exactly how far they have come and how far is left to go. Each shift marks the completion of one etak segment. When the final star aligns with the reference island, landfall is close.

The reference island is not a fiction. It is a different cognitive frame. The navigator is not pretending the island moves. They are working in a coordinate system where the observer is fixed and the world's repositioning is the information.

David Lewis documented the method in his 1972 book *We, the Navigators*. He noted that Captain Cook, one of the most accomplished Western navigators in history, sailed with Tupaia, a Polynesian navigator who could name and locate over a hundred islands. Cook never understood how Tupaia knew what he knew. Cook saw the results. He could not see the system.

## Why this name

Lucy Suchman's 1987 book *Plans and Situated Actions* argued that plans are not scripts. They are resources for action. Real work is always situated. It emerges from, and responds to, the specific circumstances in which it unfolds. Her target was AI planning systems that assumed a plan could fully specify an action in advance. Her counter-model was the river paddler who reads the water as they go, not the cartographer who reads the map before getting in the boat.

The Polynesian navigator is Suchman's argument made concrete. They do not follow a route. They maintain a rich model of the space and continuously reorient based on what the environment is telling them. The plan is a resource. The orientation is the work.

Product development works this way too, or should. Customer needs are not a route. Markets are not roads. The space a product team operates in is multi-dimensional, responsive, and changing. Teams that navigate it well do not follow roadmaps as scripts. They maintain orientation. They read the signals. They reorient when the signals change.

The tools most product teams use treat every deviation from the plan as a failure to be resolved rather than information to be learned. Etak is built on the opposite premise: that figuring out where you are, in relation to the customer and the problem, is the skill that matters. The rest is execution.

## The concept in one sentence

A product team's job is not to follow a route. It is to stay oriented in a space where the islands are moving.
